LANGUE ET CULTURE 1

The Vietnamese and France

French intervention in Southeast Asia goes back to the early–19th Century, when Asian converts to Christianity appealed to France to protect them from persecution. By 1887, after suppressing local uprisings and battles with the Chinese, who had attempted to claim areas of Southeast Asia, the French set up the colony of Indochine, which included Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia.

In 1942, while Vietnam was under Japanese occupation, Ho Chi Minh founded an independence movement sponsored by the communists. From 1946 until the  of the Battle of Diên Biên Phu in 1954, the French fought to maintain their hold over Vietnam. In 1954 French colonial rule ended, and Vietnam was split into the Communist north and the Republic of South Vietnam. After Viet Cong insurgents put the independence of South Vietnam at risk, John F. Kennedy and the U.S. sent advisors and then troops to support the government of South Vietnam against the Communists, which lead to the Vietnam War.

French cultural influence was strong in Vietnam, Cambodia, and Laos, and many Indochinese had ties to France. For this reason, many Vietnamese fled to France to escape the communists, and formed a community in Paris, most notably in the 13th arrondissement. The Vietnamese are one of the largest components of the Asian community in France, and Vietnamese restaurants can be found throughout.
