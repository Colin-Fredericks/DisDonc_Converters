Colonialism's Legacy I: A France Less European,

Less Christian, More Racist

Since decolonization, it is evident that the French population and its values have greatly changed. The primary reason for this change is that the French population, which fifty years ago was largely Catholic and European, has changed due to the significant immigration that it experienced in the 1960s and 1970s.

After the decolonization of France's last big colony, Algeria, General de Gaulle (then president of France) wanted to launch the country into a movement of economic development, modernization, and industrialization. Nevertheless, the available French work force was declining, and it was necessary to call upon the manpower of immigrants from France's former colonies, notably North and Central Africa. The number of immigrants from these countries who came to France was large, and the issue of language did not pose a problem because French is one of the official languages - often the only official language - of many countries in North and Central Africa. Until 1975, only men were able to go work in France for a few years in the hope of returning to their native countries some time later with more money. But this posed a number of humanitarian problems, and in 1975 the French government accepted family regrouping; in other words, the families of workers were also permitted to immigrate to France. With the arrival of an economic crisis at the same time, all immigration for work purposes was forbidden due to unemployment. It was at this time that there was a transition in France from a work-related immigration to one of foreigners coming to live in France and become French.

It is necessary to recognize that France had not been a land of immigration, contrary, for example, to the United States. It was consequently ill-prepared to acquire and integrate a population of foreigners. There had previously been an immigration of peoples of European descent (Italian, Spanish, Belgian), but not one of peoples whose cultures were more different and remote. These immigrants soon found themselves on the bottom rung of French society because of their cultural and physical differences. The major problem was and remains a religious one: Islam quickly became the second religion in France, currently with three million followers there. Although France does not have an official religion, the society was founded on Catholic values; this created an obvious tension between the values of the immigrant populations and those shared by the majority of the French. Furthermore, because of the economic crisis, the immigrants were first to fall into obscurity and poverty, living in poor suburbs that are often compared to ghettos. In brief, French society has proven incapable of truly integrating and accepting these populations. In the 1980s, a number of racist movements emerged; notably, le Front national gathered a large following in regions where there was a large immigrant population.

It seems that today integration is occurring gradually. The second and third generations of immigrants have many fewer difficulties in finding work or attending top schools. For example, a couple of years ago a second generation Algerian received first place in the entrance exam to the École normale supérieure, one of the most prestigious and competitive schools in France. Another immigrant who has attracted a lot of positive attention in the past few years is Zidane, a soccer player who is the son of Algerian immigrants from Marseille. The examples are infinite, but big problems exist and sometimes occasionally create a true identity crisis for French society. Recently, a controversial debate occurred over the issue of young Muslim girls who wear a veil to school, because this is considered a religious symbol that conflicts with the secular nature of the French public school system. These young girls were therefore sent home, an act that consequently elicited a terrible reaction from the Muslim community.

The presence in France of these new populations is therefore seen as a true challenge for the society and as a reminder of a colonial heritage that no one can deny.
