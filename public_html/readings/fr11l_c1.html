LANGUE ET CULTURE 1: SUR LES CANALS ET DANS LES RUES

Le Café au Relais du Canal

When Régine refers to the Café au Relais du Canal, the word canal in the name of the café refers to the Canal de l’Ourcq. The Canal de l'Ourcq links the Ourcq River, in northern France, with the city of Paris. The Canal de l'Ourcq enters the city through the northeastern neighborhoods at La Villette, where it merges with the Canal St. Martin, which connects with the Seine. The Canal de l'Ourcq is 108 kilometers long.

The serpentine Canal St. Martin was built during the Restoration (1814–1830), a period of restored monarchy that followed the French Revolution. The Canal St. Martin is 4.8 kilometers long, including a 1.8 kilometer segment that passes through a tunnel. It meets the Seine at Arsenal, which is now a popular dock for pleasure boats.

Rue Jomard

The rue Jomard is a small street in the 19th arrondissement, in northeastern Paris. Traditionally a working-class, industrial area, the entire arrondissement has undergone a vast urban renewal, spurred by the perennial Paris housing shortage. The creation of the national science museum, La cité des Sciences et de l’Industrie, has also attracted people to this part of Paris. The museum's symbol is the huge polished steel sphere called “La Géode,” which measures 36 meters in diameter.

Le Métro

In the Conversation, Éliane, Paul, and Daniel go to the café by taking the Métro Crimée. The Paris subway system, Le Métro (short for Le Métropolitain), links every neighborhood of the city. The Métro was the idea of French engineer Fulgence Bienvenüe (1852–1936), who also supervised its construction. The first line was opened in 1900; it runs in an east–west direction from the Porte de Maillot to the Bois de Vincennes, passing under the Champs-Élysées. When giving addresses in the city of Paris, people often mention the nearest subway station. Crimée (rue de Crimée) is on Ligne 7 (Villejuif-La Courneuve).

Les rues de Paris

Paris street names commemorate famous people, French and foreign cities, and events in history. Crimée means Crimea, the region of southern Russia where France, Britain, and the Turks fought against the Russians from 1854 to 1856. This was the war in which Florence Nightingale, the founder of modern nursing, became famous for her work among wounded soldiers. Two of the most famous battles of the war, the Alma River and Sébastopol, are also commemorated in Paris street names: the Place de l’Alma and the Boulevard de Sébastopol.

Faire les courses

Urban neighborhoods in France offer many small specialty food shops, such as bakeries, pastry shops, butcher shops, dairies, and open-air markets, for daily shopping. However, most French food shopping is done in supermarkets, and convenience foods play a large role in food preparation, just as in the United States. When Éliane says, “Je descends faire les courses,” she is probably going to a supermarket. The expression faire les courses is used in the context of supermarkets and stores, while faire le marché is used for shopping at open-air markets.
