LANGUE ET CULTURE 1

Le bol

In French homes, breakfast coffee is usually served in a bowl without handles and is usually black and very strong. most people add sugar, but not milk or cream. The traditional French breakfast consists of coffee, and bread or croissants with butter or jam. Meat and eggs are usually not eaten in the morning. In recent decades, American cereals have become popular, especially with children.

Le croissant

These flaky French crescent rolls have become staples of the American pastry repertoire over the last 20 years. The word croissant has an interesting history. Although most people think of croissants as quintessentially French, they first came to France from Vienna in the 19th century. The French word croissant is a translation of the German word Hörnchen. Croissants were created in Vienna to celebrate the victory over the Turks in 1689. The shape of the pastry imitates the crescent moon, the Turkish national symbol.

Le ticket de Métro

This borrowing from English is usually limited to tickets used on buses and subways. For most other kinds of tickets, French uses the word le billet. The word “ticket” is a good example of how English and French vocabularies influence each other. In the Conversation and Un peu plus sections you will see the French words croissant, rendez-vous, message, bizarre, and station, all of which have found their way into English. Likewise, the French word céréale, which always existed in the language, has now acquired from English the meaning breakfast cereal.

Stalingrad

The battle of Stalingrad was fought between Germany and the Soviet Union during World War II. This long and bloody confrontation lasted from September of 1942 to February 2, 1943, when the German troops surrendered to the Russians. Stalingrad was the turning point of the war on the Russian front, and marked the beginning of the slow and bitter fight to drive the invading Nazis from Russia. The city of Stalingrad, located on the Volga River, is today called Volgograd.

Gare de l’Est

The Gare de l’Est is one of the largest railway stations in Paris. Trains leave from the Gare de l'Est for Central and Eastern Europe.

La direction

Métro lines, although officially numbered, are more commonly referred to by their terminal stations. Thus, the line that stops at Crimée is number 7, but is called La Courneuve-Villejuif to indicate the final stops at either end. On the platforms the signs say Direction La Courneuve or Direction Villejuif to specify the direction in which the train is traveling.

Bobigny

Bobigny is a suburban city just northeast of Paris. It is a center of the French automobile industry.

Place de la République

Every French city and town has its own Place de la République. The Paris Place de la République is a huge square that was laid out in the eastern part of the city in 1854 by Baron Georges Haussmann, whose major construction projects transformed the city of Paris. Haussmann planned the large avenues branching out from the Place de la Répulique to be able to carry troops into this rough and insurrection-prone part of working-class Paris.
