Le Maghreb

French North Africa is often called by its Arabic name le Maghreb and people from North Africa are maghrébins (feminine: maghrébine). The Arab population of France (including the French-born children of the immigrants) is estimated at over 4,000,000. One of the characters in the Conversation, Karim Halibi is a maghrébin.

France has a large Arab and Muslim population, most of whom come from the country's former North African colonies: Morocco (le Maroc), Algeria (l’Algérie), and Tunisia (la Tunisie). French colonization in Africa dates back to the 1800's when French forces captured the city of Algiers (Alger). By 1848, the Algerian coast had been divided into départements, and was considered part of France. Tunisia became a French protectorate in 1881, Morocco in 1912.

Decolonization came relatively easily to Morocco and Tunisia, which became independent in 1956. In Algeria, however, the struggle for independence was much more difficult. Algeria was considered part of France and had a French population of nearly one million. The French colonists had strong ties to Algeria and were reluctant to give up their claims. The resistance against Algerian independence led to the bloody Algerian War, 1954 – 1962 (la Guerre d’Algérie). The war resulted in 300,000 to 600,000 deaths and led to the abandonment of Algeria by its French population.
