The University System in France

Today, more than ever, the French university system is under scrutiny from both political and educational perspectives. One major complaint regarding the system is that since the late 19th century, when it was first created, the university system has undergone little change. Unlike secondary education in France, which has maintained a formidable reputation worldwide, the university system in France has been criticized frequently.

Contrary to universities in most industrialized countries, French universities are public and open to all. Any student who has passed the baccalauréat can, if he or she wishes, enroll in the university of his or her choice. In the first year, the student chooses a subject in which he or she will specialize; upon completion of his/her studies, the student receives a diploma. The tuition in French universities is virtually free - the student must generally pay registration fees of around 800 French francs (the equivalent of $130). The fees for the university are entirely paid by the state. From the 1800s to the 1950s, the university system in France functioned very well because the number of students enrolled in the university was very limited.

In the past 30 years, studies have become progressively more democratic. The number of applicants to the universities has increased rapidly, and, as a result, universities have found themselves unable to effectively serve a massive number of with more personalized attention. French universities have not been well prepared for the influx of applicants, most of whom pursue advanced degrees in order to obtain better jobs. The number of students is so great that university diplomas like the Diplôme d'études universitaires générales (DEUG) or the Licence (degree following the DEUG and preceding the Maîtrise) no longer have the same value in the job market, even though the level of education remains at the highest level in most French universities. Successive French governments have refused to reform the system or institute a system to counteract student unions that are very influential in driving educational and political debates. Universities are therefore condemned for lacking a means to resolve these issues.

In response to this problem in the university system, preparatory classes have been developed to seek out academically advanced students. These prep courses are classes in which the best students prepare for entrance exams for prestigious universities, such as l'École polytechnique and l'École normale supérieure. The number of students admitted in these classes is very limited and the selection of students by the university is based on their performance. Once admitted, students study in small classes. These schools are not universities; they offer a superior level of education and are generally public and free. The prestige of these schools and of those who attend them is such that graduates generally have little difficulty finding good employment. The feeling at these schools is very different from that of the university - the competition and the work are exceptionally rigorous.

The French university system is composed of two systems with very different sentiments: on the one hand, there is the university, which offers education to a large majority of students; on the other hand, there are schools that tend to be more elitist and offer education to an extremely limited number of students. The construction of the university system is a subject of enormous debate in the political world because the selective way in which students are picked for the more prestigious schools is perceived as unfair. Hence, the subject of the French university system is a very sensitive one that the French government has yet to resolve.
