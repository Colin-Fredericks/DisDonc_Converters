LANGUE ET CULTURE

“On descend faire le marché?”

French food shopping has undergone major changes since World War II (WW II). Traditionally, French people shopped for food in small specialty shops. For example, the boulangerie made only bread and rolls, the pâtisserie offered only pastries, and the boucherie and charcuterie sold fresh meats. Prior to WWII Shopping was done daily and sometimes before each meal since these small specialty shops were located in all parts of cities and towns.

The revolution in mass merchandizing has since made the supermarket, le supermarché, and the large superstore, l’hypermarché, the mainstay of French food shopping. Part of the switch is also due to the rapid growth of suburbs around the major cities such as Paris, Lyons, and Marseilles. Small stores still exist in the cities and towns, but there are fewer than before. Also, combinations of small shops are increasingly common: boulangerie-pâtisserie or the boucherie-charcuterie.

“Je mets tout ça dans mon filet.”

The filet (literally, net) is a mesh bag that French women typically use when they shop for a day’s food in the city by going to several specialty shops.

Un kilo / une livre

All French measurements (weight, volume, length, liquid, and temperature) are expressed in the metric system. The base unit of weight is le gramme (the gram) and is equivalent to about 1/26 of an ounce. The French took the lead in setting up standards of weights and measures after the French Revolution (1789). One of the first attempts (1795) was to set the meter at a fraction of the distance between Dunkerque, England on the northern coast of France and the Spanish city of Barcelona. A year later the meter was set at 1/10,000,000 of the earth’s surface between the North Pole and the Equator.

The word kilo is short for kilogramme, or one thousand grams. There are .4536 kilogrammes in a pound (1 pound = 453.6 grams). Thus, un kilo is about two pounds in weight. The word une livre (a pound) should not be confused with un livre, (a book). It is widely used in France and means half a kilo, or about 1 U.S. pounds as in, “Une livre de pommes, s’il vous plaît.”
