A Government Still Very Much a Force in the Economy

 

Among the Western democracies, one of the characteristics that distinguishes France from the others is that the government regulates the economy and is also the major player in it.  The centralizing, Colbertian tradition of an all-powerful government is still very much alive, even if it is gradually being weakened under the influence of the European Union in particular and of popular discontent in the face of the government’s opposition to progress where institutional reforms are concerned.

 

Numerous sectors are still run by the government. The majority of education in France is public at all levels, and the most prestigious schools are public. (Moreover, they are the schools in which civil servants are educated!)  The French are devoted to this system, for it is perceived as being a factor in the equalization of opportunities.  The same goes for the field of health, where most hospitals and the medical insurance system (Social Security) are public.  However, an aging population and more and more expensive care are making modernization of the structure of this sector necessary, although it is slow in coming about since it would be politically very risky. The government is also at the heart of the retirement system, which is based on the principle of sharing – the pensions of retirees are financed by the working population. As has been known for a long time, the system is not viable at mid-term for demographic reasons, but a serious reform introducing a funded pension plan and lengthening the time for paying into the system is still a long way off.

 

France is also remarkable for its redistribution system, which is very generous compared to American standards. At the end of the 1980s, the Revenu Minimum d’Insertion (income support system) (RMI) was created for an amount of approximately 400 euros per month for a single person, paid out to persons of 25 years and older who are without resources.  Housing allotments are also paid to those whose incomes are too small to permit them to pay for housing, and so-called “family” allotments are paid to parents with dependent children.  France also has a very active public housing policy through the Habitations á Loyer Modéré (moderate rental housing) (HLM), intended for low-income households.

 

When he became president of the republic in 1981, François Mitterand shocked the nation by nationalizing numerous large companies. Since 1986, those same businesses, one after the other, have been re-privatized, thus diminishing the direct power of the government in industrial matters. Today, the government remains the owner of a few banks and medium-sized insurance companies, the majority of national transportation infrastructures (railways, highways, ports, etc.); and the largest producer of electricity in the world, Electricité de France (EDF); and it is a majority shareholder in several partially privatized businesses such as France Telecom. Continuing with privatization will allow for reduction of the government debt load or for financing the retirement system.

 

The greatest counterbalance to the French state is or soon will be the European Union, which, in addition to monetary power, will from now on concentrate on regulatory power concerning competition.  Thus, Government monopolies of telephone services or electricity will have to be abolished.  In the areas where the government remains in charge in principle, particularly in fiscal matters, there is increasing talk of the need for conformity with the rest of Europe.  On the whole, a more liberal consensus is appearing in France, as witnessed by the recent acknowledgment of the Socialist Prime Minister that, faced with massive layoffs in some industries, “The government can’t do everything."
