3. Prononciation

You have seen that certain final consonants in French are written but not pronounced unless the next word begins with a vowel. This phenomenon is known as liaison. Compare the following two sentences:

Il est professeur.
C'est un professeur.

In the first sentence, the final t of estis not pronounced because the following word begins with a consonant. In the second sentence, the final t of estis pronounced because the following word begins with vowel. Listen to the sentences again.

Il est professeur.
C'est un professeur.

Note that the t of c'est is pronounced as if it were the first consonant of the following syllable: c'es - tun.

La Structure de la Langue 1

Le verbe faire

The verb faire (to do, to make) is irregular.

Je fais des sciences politiques.
Tu fais des sciences politiques.
Il/Elle/On fait des sciences politiques.
Nous faisons des sciences politiques.
Vous faites des sciences politiques.
Ils/Elles font des sciences politiques.

The vowels in the singular forms of faire are pronounced like the è in très in formal speech, but like the é in café in everyday usage. The first vowel sound in the nous form, faisons, is pronounced like the e in je. This is not a typical pronunciation for the letters ai in French. In the vous form, faites, the ai is also pronounced like the è in très, one of the typical pronunciations for this combination of letters. With these distinctions in mind, listen to the forms of faire again.

La Structure de la Langue 2: L’ARTICLE PARTITIF

In addition to the definite and indefinite articles, French has a third article, the partitive. The partitive article is used before mass nouns, which are nouns that express quantities that cannot be counted. Look at the following nouns:

l’informatique
computer science
la salade
salad
la musique
music
le pâté
pâté

These nouns usually don’t appear with numbers in front of them, nor do they usually have a plural form. They are called “mass nouns,” as opposed to other nouns like livre, sac, and ville, which are called “count nouns” because they can be counted and have a plural form. Nouns referring to food, such as saladeand pâté, can be counted if they refer to servings of the food or varieties of the food: deux salades means “two salads” (i.e., two servings of salad).

Look at the following English sentences:

I study computer science.
We’re eating salad.
She listens to classical music.
I have pâté.

What article or determiner is used before the mass nouns computer science, salad, classical music, and pâté?

Show Answer

No article is used.

Now listen to the French equivalents of the above sentences:

Je fais de l’informatique.
Nous mangeons de la salade.
Elle écoute de la musique classique.
J’ai du pâté.

Questions:

  1. What do you see and hear before the mass nouns?
  2. What form of the partitive is used before masculine singular nouns beginning with a consonant?
  3. What form of the partitive is used before feminine singular nouns beginning with a consonant?
  4. What form of the partitive is used before any singular noun beginning with a vowel?
Show Answers
  1. Combinations of de plus the definite article. The partitive article in French consists of de plus the definite article. The highlighted words are the partitive articles.
  2. Du, as in the sentence: “J’ai du pâté.”
  3. De la, as in the sentences: “Nous mangeons de la salade.” and “Elle écoute de la musique classique.”
  4. De l’, as in the sentence: “Je fais de l’informatique.”

In the plural, the partitive merges with the plural of the indefinite article.

Il apporte des bonbons.
He’s bringing candy.
Elle fait des sciences.
She’s studying science.

La Structure de la Langue 4: IL EST/ELLE EST VS. C’EST

To specify a person’s profession, nationality, or religion, il est, elle est, or c’estis used.

If the word designating the profession, nationality, or religion is unmodified, in other words, if it is not accompanied by a determiner or an adjective, then il est / elle est is used (ils sont / elles sont in the plural).

Il est professeur.
He’s a teacher.
Elle est ingénieur.
She’s an engineer.
Ils sont protestants.
They’re Protestant(s).
Elles sont chinoises.
They’re Chinese.

Note that in the above examples, the nouns professeur and ingénieur function in the same way as the adjectives protestants and chinoises — no article is used before them. Also note that protestants and chinoises are written with a lowercase initial letter to indicate that they are adjectives.

Listen to the following sentences.

C’est un professeur excellent.
He/She is an excellent teacher.
C’est un ingénieur électricien.
He/She is an electrical engineer.
Ce sont des Protestants pratiquants.
They are observant Protestants.
Ce sont des Chinoises.
They are Chinese.

Questions:

  1. How do the sentences with C’est / Ce sont differ from those with Il est / Elle est and Ils sont / Elles sont?
  2. What happens to adjectives of religion and nationality such as protestant and chinois in these sentences? Why?
  3. What article is used in the plural sentences?
Show Answers
  1. In the sentences with C’est / Ce sont, the words for nationality, religion, and profession are modified (they are accompanied by a determiner and/or by an adjective and therefore they function as nouns).
  2. The adjectives of religion and nationality, such as protestant and chinois, are capitalized in these sentences because they function as nouns.
  3. Des, the plural of the indefinite article is used after ce sont: Ce sont des Protestants pratiquants. Ce sont des Chinoises.

In colloquial speech, c’est often replaces ce sont before plural nouns:

C’est des ingénieurs.
They’re engineers.
C’est mes voisins.
They’re my neighbors.
C’est nos professeurs.
They’re our teachers.

La Structure de la Langue 6

How to Express “Have Been Doing Something”

Look at the following English sentences:

We have been studying French for two months.
They have been working in Paris for a year.

Questions:

  1. Is a simple or compound tense used in these sentences?
  2. Can you describe the time frame for the actions that these constructions convey?
Show Answers
  1. A compound tense is used: have + been + studying.
  2. They express actions that began in the past but that are continuing into the present.

Now look at the French equivalents of the above sentences:

Il y a deux mois que nous faisons du français.
Il y a un an qu’ils travaillent à Paris.

Questions:

  1. What tense is used in the French sentences to express the time frame of the action?
  2. What word is used in the English sentences to mark the period of time that the action has been going on?
  3. What words are used in the French sentences to mark the period of time that the action has been going on?
Show Answers
  1. The present tense is used.
  2. The preposition for is used: for two months...; for a year...
  3. Il y a precedes the time expression and que follows it: Il y a deux mois que...; Il y a un an que...

The French sentences can be phrased another way:

Nous faisons du français depuis deux mois.
Ils travaillent à Paris depuis un an.

Questions:

  1. In the above sentences, the present tense is still used, but the time expression is marked differently. What word is used to mark the period of time that the action has been going on?
  2. Where is the time phrase placed in these sentences?
Show Answers
  1. Depuis. The word depuis, since, precedes the expression of time.
  2. Depuis phrases usually appear at the end of a sentence.

Here’s how to ask questions about how long actions have been going on. Note that the phrase combien de temps?, how long?, can be preceded by either il y a or depuis:

Il y a combien de temps que vous travaillez ici?
Depuis combien de temps est-ce que vous travaillez ici?

Both questions mean “How long have you been working here?” However, the constructions with il y a are more common in speech.
