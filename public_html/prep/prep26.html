La Différence Entre "Plein" Et "Complet"

Plein is the general term for full. Complet means sold out or full house and refers to hotels, trains, planes, theaters, or sports events.

La Structure De La Langue 1: Les Pronoms Interrogatifs

Listen to the following pairs of sentences:

Quelle valise s’est cassée?
Which suitcase broke?
Laquelle s’est cassée?
Which one broke?

Questions:

  1. What is the subject of the first sentence?
  2. What is the subject of the second sentence?
  3. What part of speech is "quelle"?
  4. What part of speech is "laquelle"?
Show Answers
  1. The subject of the first sentence is valise.
  2. The subject of the second sentence is laquelle.
  3. Quelle is an adjective. It modifies valise.
  4. Laquelle is a pronoun. It replaces quelle valise and functions like a noun, filling the role of subject of the sentence in this case.

Note that the interrogative pronoun in French agrees in gender and number with the noun it replaces.

La Structure De La Langue 2: Les Pronoms Démonstratifs

Remember that the French demonstrative adjectives do not distinguish "this"from "that" or "these" from "those."

Cette valise à roulettes est très chère.
This/That suitcase on wheels is very expensive.
Ces guichets sont fermés.
These/Those ticket windows are closed.

To specify between "this" and "that," and "these" and "those," formal French uses the suffixes ci and là. The English equivalents of these forms have contrastive stress on the demonstratives, as is illustrated in the examples below.

Ce train-ci est complet.
This train is full.
Ce train-là a encore des places.
That train still has seats.
Ces places-ci sont réservées.
These seats are reserved.
Ces places-là sont libres.
Those seats are free.

However, in spoken French, là replaces ci, and there is no contrast between "this"and "that" or "these" and "those."

Ce porte-habits-là est trop lourd.
This/That garment bag is too heavy.
Ces trousses-là sont trop petites.
These/Those toiletry bags are too small.

The suffix ci is only used when the contrast between "this"and "that" (or "these"and "those") is the focus of the sentence.

Cette sacoche-ci est pleine.
This satchel is full.
Cette sacoche-là est vide.
That satchel is empty.

Now look at the following sentences:

Ce peigne est à toi?
Is this/that comb yours?
Non, celui-là n’est pas le mien.
No, this one / that one is not mine.

Madame, s’il vous plaît. J’ai besoin d’une brosse à cheveux.
Excuse me, Ma’am. I need a hairbrush.
Celle-là est très bonne.
This one / That one is very good.

Tu vas acheter quels magazines?
Which magazines are you going to buy?
Je lis toujours ceux-là.
I always read these/those.

Toutes ces places sont réservées?
Are all these/those seats reserved?
Non, celles-là sont libres. Vous pouvez vous y asseoir.
No. These/Those are free. You can sit there.

Questions:

  1. What functions do the words celui-là, celle-là, ceux-là, and celles-là perform in the above sentences?
  2. What part of speech are these words?
Show Answers
  1. In the above sentences, celui-là, celle-là, and celles-là function as the subject of their sentences. Ceux-là is the direct object of the verb of its sentence.
  2. The words celui-là, celle-là, ceux-là, and celles-là are pronouns called demonstrative pronouns. They are used to replace a phrase consisting of a demonstrative adjective + noun when the noun is understood, but not expressed. As pronouns, they can perform the structural roles of nouns: they can function as the subject or object of a verb.

Note that ci can be used with demonstrative pronouns to focus on the contrast between "this"and "that."

Ces deux trousses sont chères. Celle-ci est en cuir, celle-là est en nylon.
These two toiletry cases are expensive. This one is made of leather; that one is made of nylon.

La Structure De La Langue 3: Les Pronoms Démonstratifs Dans Les Constructions Possessifs Et Relatifs Et Les Pronoms Ceci, Cela

Demonstrative pronouns are used to replace nouns in possessive constructions before the preposition de and before relative clauses. Study the following sentences. Notice the English equivalents of these forms.

Toi et Jean-Luc, vous habitez au même étage?
Do you and Jean-Luc live on the same floor?
Non, mon appartement est au premier.
No, my apartment is on the first floor.

Ma situation est difficile.
My situation is difficult.
Celle d’Éliane est encore plus difficile.
Éliane’s is even harder.

Notre site Web est très facile et très pratique.
Our Web site is very easy and very practical.
Mais ceux de nos concurrents sont compliqués et très difficiles à utiliser.
But our competitors’ are complicated and very hard to use.

Où sont les disquettes?
Where are the disks?
La police se charge de celles d’Éliane.
The police are holding Éliane’s.

Il y a un livre sur la table. C’est ce livre-là que tu veux?
There’s a book on the table. Is that the book you want?
Non, je veux celui qui est sur la chaise.
No, I want the one that’s on the table.

Qui est la stagiaire qui ne travaille plus ici?
Who is the intern who doesn’t work here any longer?
Celle qu’on vient de mettre à la porte s’appelle Éliane.
The one they’ve just fired is named Éliane.

Je trouve que nous avons de bons informaticiens.
I think we have good computer specialists.
Oui, ceux qu’on vient d’embaucher sont excellents.
Yes, the ones we have just hired are excellent.

La France a d’excellentes autoroutes.
France has excellent superhighways.
Celles que j’ai prises étaient excellentes.
The ones I took were excellent.

French has two additional demonstrative pronouns: ceci, this, and cela,that. Ceci and cela (colloquially, ça) don’t refer to specific things, but to situations. In spoken French, ceci is less common and is replaced by cela.

Ça vous embête de prendre le train?
Does it bother you to take the train?
Non, au contraire, j’aime ça.
No, quite the contrary. I like it.

La Structure De La Langue 4: Les Pronoms Possessifs

Both French and English have a set of possessive pronouns that replace possessive adjectives when the noun does not appear.

Elle lit son livre?
Is she reading her book?
Non, elle ne lit pas le sien. Elle lit le mien.
No, she’s not reading hers. She’s reading mine.

Tu aimes ton eau de cologne?
Do you like your cologne?
Oui, j’aime la mienne, mais je préfère la tienne.
Yes, I like mine, but I like yours better.

Questions:

  1. How do French possessive pronouns differ from their English equivalents?
  2. Can you guess the French masculine plural form of "mine"? Can you guess the French feminine plural form of "mine"?
  3. What two components are there in a French possessive pronoun?
Show Answers
  1. French possessive pronouns agree in gender and number with the nouns they replace. English marks the gender of the possessor (his vs. hers ). Le sien can mean either his or hers , but refers to a masculine singular noun (in the first pair of sentences, le sien refers to livre).
  2. The masculine plural form for "mine" is les miens. The feminine plural form for "mine" is les miennes.
  3. A French possessive pronoun consists of the definite article and a possessive form. Both the article and the possessive form agree in gender and number with the noun being replaced.

In the singular (mine, yours, his, hers, its), French possessive pronouns have four forms of the possessive component.

Masculine Singular Feminine Singular Masculine Plural Feminine Plural
Mine le mien la mienne les miens les miennes
Yours le tien la tienne les tiens les tiennes
His le sien la sienne les siens les siennes

In the plural, French possessive pronouns show only number agreement.

Masculine Singular Feminine Singular Masculine Plural Feminine Plural
Ours le nôtre la nôtre les nôtres les nôtres
Yours le vôtre la vôtre les vôtres les vôtres
Theirs le leur la leur les leurs les leurs

Note that the pronominal forms nôtre and vôtre are not only written with a circumflex accent, but pronounced with closed o, while the possessive adjectives notre and votre are pronounced with open o.

Note that phrases consisting of à + stressed pronoun are often the equivalents of possessive pronouns with être.

Ce livre est le tien?
Is this book yours?
Ce livre est à toi?
Is this book yours?

La Structure De La Langue 5A: Tout: Révision Et Expansion

The word tout can be used as an adjective, a pronoun, and an adverb. When used as an adjective, tout has four forms and is usually followed by the definite article. Examine the following sentences. Listen and repeat, paying special attention to the pronunciation of the forms of tout .

They lost all my suitcases.

Questions:

  1. When used as an adjective, how many forms does tout have in written French?
  2. When used as an adjective, how many forms does tout have in spoken French?
Show Answers

1. Tout, when used as an adjective, has four forms in written French:

Singular Plural
Masculine tout tous
Feminine toute toutes

2. When used as an adjective, tout has two forms in spoken French. The feminine singular and plural forms (toute, toutes) are pronounced alike and end in a pronounced t. That final t sound drops in the masculine singular and plural forms (tout, tous) that end in the vowel sound ou.

Questions:

  1. How does the pronunciation of tous as a pronoun differ from the pronunciation of tous as an adjective?
  2. What does the expression tout le monde mean? How does it differ from tous?
Show Answers
  1. When tous is a pronoun, the final s is pronounced. The final s is not pronounced when it is an adjective.
  2. The expression tout le monde means everyone. Tous, however, also means all of them, and is followed by a plural verb. Tout le monde is singular (like the English everyone) and is followed by a singular verb.

La Structure De La Langue 5B: Tout: Révision Et Expansion

Tout can be used before adjectives as an intensifier meaning very or completely. Adverbial tout is pronounced tou (final t is silent) before masculine singular adjectives beginning with a consonant.

Il était tout satisfait de son travail.
He was completely satisfied with his work.

There are cases where the final t of tout is pronounced.

1. The final t of tout is pronounced before masculine and feminine adjectives beginning with a vowel or a mute h, whether the adjective is singular or plural.

La jeune femme était tout heureuse / tout optimiste.
The young woman was very happy / completely optimistic.

2. Tout with the final t pronounced is written toute before feminine singular adjectives beginning with a consonant and toutes before feminine plural adjectives beginning with a consonant.

Mme Jolivet est toute contente.
Mrs. Jolivet is very happy.
La valise d’Éliane était toute neuve.
Éliane’s suitcase was brand new.
Les valises d’Éliane étaient toutes neuves.
Éliane’s suitcases were brand new.
Régine et Éliane sont toutes courageuses.
Régine and Éliane are very brave.

La Structure De La Langue 6: Les Noms Composés Avec La Préposition À

The preposition à is used to form compound nouns. It has several meanings:

1. It indicates the primary ingredient:

un sandwich au jambon
a ham sandwich
une glace au chocolat
chocolate ice cream

2. It labels the defining characteristic:

l’homme à l’imperméable gris
the man in the gray raincoat
le train à six voitures
the train with six cars
une valise à roulettes
a suitcase on wheels

3. It labels the purpose for which the main noun is intended:

une brosse à dents
a toothbrush
de la crème à raser
shaving cream

Note the difference:

une tasse de thé
a cup of tea
une tasse à thé
a teacup
