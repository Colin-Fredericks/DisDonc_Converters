LA STRUCTURE DE LA LANGUE 2: LE COMPARATIF DES ADJECTIFS

Comparisons with adjectives involve three structures in French:

plus + adjective + que

moins + adjective + que

aussi + adjective + que

The conjunction que is the equivalent of than or as in comparative constructions.

Écoutez.

  

Une veste est plus pratique qu’un anorak.

A sport jacket is more practical than a ski jacket.

Ce bistrot est moins cher que le restaurant.

This bistro is less expensive than the restaurant.

Ce magasin est aussi élégant que la boutique.

This store is as elegant as the shop.

Note that the most common English translation for moins + adjective + que is not as + adjective + as.

Écoutez.

  

L’absence de M. Toësca est moins importante que les deux petits bouts de papier.

Mr. Toësca’s absence is not as important as the two little pieces of paper.

Paul et Daniel sont moins forts que M. Toësca.

Paul and Daniel are not as strong as Mr. Toësca.

L’autocar est moins rapide que le TGV.

The bus is not as fast as the TGV.

Note that stressed pronouns are used after que in comparisons.

Écoutez.

  

Mon cousin est plus fort en maths que moi.

My cousin is better in math than I am.

Je suis aussi pressé que toi.

I am as much in a hurry as you are.

Il est moins inquiet qu’eux.

He is less worried than they are.

Now look at the following sentences.

Écoutez.

  

Cette valise est meilleure que l’autre.

This suitcase is better than the other one.

M. Toësca est pire que nos rivaux.

Mr. Toësca is worse than our rivals.

What are the French equivalents of the irregular comparative forms “better” and “worse”?

LA STRUCTURE DE LA LANGUE 3: DEUX PRONOMS COMPLÉMENTS D’OBJET

In French, it is very common for a direct and indirect object pronoun to appear together in the same sentence. In English, the indirect object pronoun is usually preceded by the preposition “to” or “for”.

Where are the reports?

I sent them to him.

How come we have his suitcase?

He gave it to us.

In French, when two object pronouns occur together, me, te, nous, and vous precede le, la, and les. In other words, the indirect object pronoun precedes the direct object pronoun.

Écoutez.

  

Tu as un nouvel imperméable?

Do you have a new raincoat?

Oui, je vais te le montrer.

Yes, I’m going to show it to you.

Écoutez.

  

Tu n’a pas les logiciels?

Don’t you have the software programs?

Non, les informaticiens ne me les ont pas donnés.

No, the computer people didn’t give them to me.

Écoutez.

  

J’ai besoin de ces billets.

I need those tickets.

Je vous les enverrai.

I’ll send them to you.

When both the direct object pronoun and the indirect object pronoun refer to the third person, le, la, and les precede lui and leur. In other words, the direct object pronoun precedes the indirect object pronoun.

Écoutez.

  

Il voulait emprunter votre voiture, n’est-ce pas?

He wanted to borrow your car, didn’t he?

Je la lui ai prêtée.

I lent it to him.

Écoutez.

  

Les étudiants comprennent les problèmes de maths?

Do the students understand the math problems?

Oui, le prof les leur a expliqués.

Yes, the teacher explained them to them.

Écoutez.

  

Jean a besoin de son livre de biologie.

Jean needs his biology book.

Mais je le lui ai rendu hier.

But I gave it back to him yesterday.

Y and en follow the other pronouns. When they occur together, y precedes en:

Écoutez.

  

Ils étaient au cinéma?

Were they at the movies?

Oui, on les y a vus.

Yes, we saw them there.

Écoutez.

  

Les enfants prennent des vitamines?

Do the children take vitamins?

Oui, leur mère leur en donne.

Yes, their mother gives them some.

Écoutez.

  

Elle prend du thé dans la cuisine?

Is she having tea in the kitchen?

Oui, elle en prend.

Yes, she is (having some there).

Note that double object pronouns follow the same rules for position as single object pronouns.

   1.          They precede the conjugated verb:

L’argent? Je le leur rends.

   2.          They precede the infinitive in verb + infinitive constructions:

L’argent? Je vais le leur rendre.

   3.          They precede the auxiliary verb in the compound tenses:

L’argent? Je le leur ai rendu.

   4.          In the negative, the particle ne precedes the object pronouns:

L’argent? Je ne le leur rends pas.

L’argent? Je ne vais pas le leur rendre.

L’argent? Je ne le leur ai pas rendu.

Now look at the following command forms with double object pronouns.

Écoutez.

LA STRUCTURE DE LA LANGUE 4: VOCABULAIRE: LES VÊTEMENTS, LES COULEURS, LES VERBES DÉRIVÉS DES ADJECTIFS DE COULEUR

The verbs mettre, to put on, and enlever, to take off, are useful when discussing clothing.

J’ai mis ma veste.

I put on my jacket

Il a enlevé sa veste.

He took off his jacket.

Other verbs, such as porter, to wear, and essayer, to try on, are also helpful when discussing clothing.

Qu’est-ce qu’elle porte?

What is she wearing?

Elle porte une longue robe noire et un collier de diamants.

She’s wearing a long, black dress and a diamond necklace.

Elle a essayé trois robes.

She tried on three dresses.

The colors rouge, jaune, vert, bleu, noir, and gris are considered "true" adjectives because they agree in gender and number with the nouns they describe. Several color words in French are really nouns, however, such as orange, beige, and marron. These adjectives are invariable.

un chemisier orange, des chemisiers orange

an orange blouse, orange blouses

un pantalon marron, des chaussures marron

brown pants, brown shoes

The noun rose is treated as an adjective.

des chaussettes roses

pink socks

To modify an adjective of color, the words foncé, dark, and clair, light, are often used.

une chemise bleu clair

a light blue shirt

une robe vert foncé

a dark green dress

des chemisiers vert clair

light green blouses

des chaussettes bleu foncé

dark blue socks

Note that phrases with foncé and clair are invariable: there is no agreement of color, gender, or number.

The following color words form -ir verbs:

rouge

rougir

to blush; turn red

jaune

jaunir

to yellow

noir

noircir

to blacken, turn black

blanc

blanchir

to whiten, to launder (e.g. money); to bleach

pâle

pâlir

to turn pale

Some of the above verbs can be transitive (used with a direct object) or intransitive (used without a direct object). Others, like rougir and pâlir, however, are almost always intransitive.

Intransitive

La nicotine a jauni ses doigts.

Le journal a jauni.

La fumée a noirci les murs.

Le ciel a noirci.

The smoke blackened the walls.

The sky turned black.
