La Structure De La Langue 1: Le Conditionnel

The conditional tense expresses the idea of would do something if. The conditional is formed by adding the endings of the imperfect tense to the stem of the future tense. For most verbs, the stem of the future is the infinitive.

Listen to the conditional tense with the following -er verb.

Écoutez.

We would arrive as soon as possible.

Vous arriveriez le plus tôt possible.

You would arrive as soon as possible.

You would arrive as soon as possible.

Il/Elle/On arriverait le plus tôt possible.

Ils/Elles arriveraient le plus tôt possible.

He/She/One would arrive as soon as possible.

They would arrive as soon as possible.

Verbs with irregular stems form the same stem in the future and the conditional.

Avoir and savoir have conditional stems in aur:

avoir

j’aurais, tu aurais, etc.

savoir

je saurais, tu saurais, etc.

Être and faire have conditional stems in er:

être

je serais, tu serais, etc.

faire

je ferais, tu ferais, etc.

Voir and envoyer have conditional stems in err:

voir

je verrais, tu verrais, etc.

envoyer

j’enverrais, tu enverrais, etc.

Verbs with infinitives in evoir drop the oi in the conditional:

devoir

je devrais, tu devrais, etc.

recevoir

je recevrais, tu recevrais, etc.

Courir and mourir drop the i of the infinitive, leaving a double r that is pronounced in speech:

courir

je courrais, tu courrais, etc.

Other common irregular conditional stems:

aller

j’irais, tu irais, etc.

falloir

il faudrait, etc.

pouvoir

je pourrais, tu pourrais, etc.

soutenir

je soutiendrais, tu soutiendrais, etc.

valoir

il vaudrait mieux

venir

je viendrais, tu viendrais, etc.

vouloir

je voudrais, tu voudrais, etc.

Note that in everyday spoken French, the first-person singular of the future and the conditional are pronounced alike. Listen and repeat:

La Structure De La Langue 2: Les Phrases Hypothéthiques (1)

Conditional sentences tell what would happen if something were or weren’t true. They have two clauses: a clause beginning with si, if, and a main clause. Examine the following sentences. Listen and repeat.

Écoutez.

S’ils comprenaient les indices, ils iraient en Suisse.

If they understood the clues, they’d go to Switzerland.

Je lui téléphonerais si j’avais son numéro.

I’d call him if I had his number.

Si Baudoin n’était pas tellement futé, on ne l’écouterait pas.

If Baudoin weren’t so shrewd, people wouldn’t listen to him.

M. Jolivet mettrait tout le monde à la porte s’il pouvait.

Mr. Jolivet would fire everyone if he could.

1. What tenses are used in the different clauses?

2. Do the clauses occur in the same order?

Show Answers

Answers

The si- clause is in the imperfect and the main clause is in the conditional.

No, either clause may occur first in the sentence.

Note that the sentences given above are contrary to fact. The fact refers to the present.

Fact:

Écoutez.

Ils ne comprennent pas les indices. Ils ne vont pas en Suisse.

They don’t understand the clues. They are not going to Switzerland.

Condition contrary-to-fact:

Écoutez.

S’ils comprenaient les indices, ils iraient en Suisse.

If they understood the facts, they’d go to Switzerland.

Fact:

Écoutez.

Je ne lui téléphone pas. Je n’ai pas son numéro.

I’m not phoning him. I don’t have his number.

Condition contrary-to-fact:

Écoutez.

Je lui téléphonerais si j’avais son numéro.

I’d phone him if I had his number.

Fact:

Écoutez.

Baudoin est tellement futé. On l’écoute.

Baudoin is so shrewd. People listen to him.

Condition contrary-to-fact:

Écoutez.

Si Baudoin n’était pas tellement futé, on ne l’écouterait pas.

If Baudoin weren’t so shrewd, people wouldn’t listen to him.

Fact:

Écoutez.

M. Jolivet ne met pas tout le monde à la porte. Il ne peut pas.

Mr. Jolivet isn’t firing everyone. He can’t.

Condition contrary-to-fact:

Écoutez.

M. Jolivet mettrait tout le monde à la porte s’il pouvait.

Mr. Jolivet would fire everyone if he could.

La Structure De La Langue 3: Le Conditionnel Dans Le Discours Rapporté

Indirect or reported speech is used to report what someone else said, thought, knew, etc. Another person's words can be quoted directly, as illustrated in the following sentences.

Écoutez.

Il me dit, «Je viendrai demain.»

He tells me, “I’ll come tomorrow.”

Il nous demande, «Qu’est-ce que vous prendrez?»

He asks us, “What will you have?”

Indirect speech in French is used when a person’s words are quoted indirectly.

Écoutez.

Il me dit qu’il viendra demain.

He tells me that he’ll come tomorrow.

Il nous demande ce que nous prendrons.

He asks us what we’ll have.

Look at the above sentences.

1. In what tense is the main clause?

2. In what tense is the reported speech?

3. How do the two original quotes differ?

Show Answers

Answers

In both sentences, the main clause is in the present tense.

In both sentences, the reported speech is in the future tense.

One quote is a statement and the other quote is a question.

Now listen to the following sentences.

Écoutez.

Il m’a dit qu’il viendrait demain.

He told me that he’d come tomorrow.

Il nous a demandé ce que nous prendrions.

He asked us what we would have.

1. What tense is the verb in the main clause?

2. What change has taken place in the tense of the verbs of the subordinate clauses?

Show Answers

Answers

The verbs in the main clause are in the past tense (passé composé and imperfect).

The verbs of the subordinate clauses have shifted from future to conditional.

Thus, to report events stated in the future, the future tense is used if the main verb is in the present tense. If the main verb is in the passé composé, the imperfect, or the pluperfect, then the verb of the subordinate clause shifts from the future to the conditional.

Note a similar use of tenses after savoir si, to know whether:

Écoutez.

Tu sais s’il ira?

Do you know whether he’ll go?

Je ne savais pas s’il irait.

I didn’t know whether he’d go.

La Structure De La Langue 4: "Quoi" Comme Pronom Interrogatif

Question words, such as qui, où, quand, and quel, may also occur with prepositions. Examine the following sentences. Listen and repeat.

Écoutez.

Avec qui est-ce que tu manges?

Whom do you eat with?

D’où êtes-vous?

Where are you from?

Pour quand est-ce qu’il faut terminer ce travail?

When does this work have to be finished by?

Par quelle porte est-ce qu’on sort?

Which door do we leave by?

1. Where do the prepositions appear in the French sentences?

2. Where do they appear in the English sentences?

Show Answers

Answers

In the French sentences, the prepositions appear before the interrogative. In the examples given, they occur as the first element of the sentences. Notice that in informal questions where the question word may appear elsewhere in the sentence, the preposition moves with it:

Tu manges avec qui?

Vous êtes d’où?

C’est pour quand, ce travail?

On sort par quelle porte?

In the colloquial English sentences, the prepositions are often separated from their objects and appear at the end of the sentence. Formal written English may move the prepositions to a position before the interrogative:

With whom are you eating?

By which door do we leave?

13. Read the Note culturelle on the French Republic and its Symbols

Note Culturelle

 

 

The French Republic and its Symbols

 

 

Numerous symbols, firmly anchored in French mentality, are attached to the French Republic. Most of those symbols come to us directly from the French Revolution (1789), although some are still more ancient. Above all, the Republic is evoked by Marianne, the tri-color flag and, of course, the national anthem.

 

Represented with a sack of wheat at her feet, Marianne does not care about power but about the aspirations of the French people. Since 1789, Marianne is often shown wearing a Phrygian bonnet and carrying a pikestaff — a reference to her distant origins: in Antiquity, freed slaves of the Roman Empire wore the Phrygian bonnet. Marianne is portrayed as a warrior, which also recalls the Greek Athena and symbolizes the fact that freedom comes through the use of weapons. During the Napoleonic Empire (First Empire: 1804-1814) and the Restoration (1814-1830), Marianne became less significant. But her most famous representation is undoubtedly that of Eugène Delacroix, the celebrated painter of the Romantic school, in "La liberté guidant le peuple aux barricades"  (“Liberty Leading the People at the Barricades”) (1830). In 1852 Napoleon III (Second Empire, 1852-1870) replaced Marianne with his own effigy on coins and stamps. The Commune (1871) and the Third Republic (1870-1940) gave Marianne back her symbolic importance, which is still evolving. All French city halls have a bust of Marianne, often sculpted with the features of Brigitte Bardot or Catherine Deneuve. The symbolic power of Marianne is reinforced proportionately to the affirmation of the concept of the French nation.

 

Several versions exist of the interpretation of the three colors of the French flag. The most widespread belief is that of the blue as the sky of “sweet France,” the white as the lilies of kings, and the red as the blood spilled for liberty. In the armies the white flag was the insignia of the colonel who represented the king. White, therefore, becomes the color of royalty. Three days after the taking of the Bastille (July 14, 1789), Louis XVI went to greet the new city government at the Hôtel de Ville. Arriving at the square, he found the façades draped in unfamiliar flags: blue and red — that had become the colors of the capital — on either side of the royal white. The three colors thus became symbols of the French Republic but were not arranged definitively in the form of three vertical bands until 1812.

 

The French national anthem came into existence in Strasbourg, composed by Captain Rouget de Lisle to cheer on the Army of the Rhine leaving for the front at Valmy. The anthem has the name "Marseillaise" because it was sung by a group of 500 volunteers from Marseilles going to Paris to participate in the taking of the Palace of the Tuileries. Parisians, seeing those soldiers from Marseilles marching by singing those verses, gave the name of "Marseillaise" to that song. In spite of the violence of the words of this song (for example, the famous "Qu’un sang impur abreuve nos sillons"  [“May the blood of the defiler drench our land”]), it has remained dear to the hearts of the French, and the President of the Republic, Valéry Giscard D’estaing, unleashed a tempest of protests when during the 1970s he wanted the "Marseillaise" played at a slower tempo. However, although the idea of changing the words of the anthem to soften them regularly reappears, it has not yet been put into practice.

 

If the Vichy period of the Second World War is excluded, the French Republic has remained stable, and all the French acknowledge that. The symbols attached to it have gone beyond the frontiers of the country, and the national anthem, like the tricolor flag or even Marianne (undoubtedly due to the world-famous Delacroix painting), are certainly among the most easily recognizable symbols in the world.

Note Culturelle

La République française et ses symboles

À la République française s’attachent de nombreux symboles, profondément ancrés dans la mentalité française. La plupart de ces symboles nous viennent directement de la Révolution Française (1789), même si certains sont plus anciens encore. La République évoque avant tout Marianne, le drapeau tricolore, et bien entendu, l’hymne national.

Représentée avec un sac de blé à ses pieds, Marianne ne se soucie pas de puissance mais des aspirations du peuple. À partir de 1789 Marianne est souvent représentée portant un bonnet phrygien, une pique — une référence à ses origines lointaines: dans l’Antiquité ce sont les esclaves affranchis de l’empire romain qui portent le bonnet phrygien. Marianne peinte en guerrière, qui rappelle aussi l’Athéna grecque, symbolise le fait que la liberté s’acquiert par les armes. Durant l’empire napoléonien (Premier Empire: 1804-1814) et la Restauration (1814-1830), Marianne est affaiblie. Mais sa représentation la plus célèbre est sans doute celle d’Eugène Delacroix, le célèbre peintre de l’école Romantique, dans «La liberté guidant le peuple aux barricades» (1830). En 1852 Napoléon III (Second Empire, 1852-1870) la fait remplacer par sa propre effigie sur les pièces de monnaie et les timbre-poste. C’est la Commune (1871) et la Troisième République (1870-1940) qui redonnent à Marianne son importance symbolique, qui n’a cessé de grandir jusqu’à présent. Toutes les mairies françaises ont un buste de Marianne souvent gravé selon les traits de Brigitte Bardot ou Catherine Deneuve. Le pouvoir symbolique de Marianne s’est renforcé à mesure que s’affirmait l’idée de la nation française.

Il existe plusieurs versions quant à l’interprétation des trois couleurs du drapeau français. La plus répandue est celle du bleu comme le ciel de la «douce France», le blanc comme les Lys des rois, le rouge comme le sang versé pour la liberté. Dans les armées le drapeau blanc est l’insigne du colonel, qui représente le roi. Le Blanc devient donc la couleur de la royauté. Trois jours après la prise de la Bastille (14 juillet 1789), Louis XVI va saluer la nouvelle municipalité à l’Hôtel de Ville. Il arrive sur la place dont les façades sont ornées de drapeaux inconnus: le bleu et le rouge, devenus les couleurs de la capitale, encadrent le blanc royal. Les trois couleurs deviennent ainsi symboles de la République française, mais ne s’agencent définitivement sous la forme de trois bandes verticales qu’en 1812.

L’hymne national français est né à Strasbourg, composé par le Capitaine Rouget de Lisle pour encourager l’armée du Rhin en partance pour le front de Valmy. L’hymne porte le nom de Marseillaise car il fut chanté par un groupe de 500 volontaires marseillais allant à Paris pour participer à la prise du palais des Tuileries. Les Parisiens voyant défiler ces Marseillais chantant ces couplets donnèrent à ce chant le nom de «Marseillaise». Malgré la violence des paroles de ce chant (par exemple le fameux «Qu’un sang impur abreuve nos sillons»), ce chant révolutionnaire est resté cher au coeur des Français, et le Président de la République Valéry Giscard D’estaing a déclenché une tempête de protestations en voulant dans les années 1970 jouer la Marseillaise sur un tempo plus lent. Cependant l’idée de changer les paroles de l’hymne pour les adoucir revient régulièrement, sans avoir encore été mise en pratique.

Si l’on exclut la période de Vichy de la Deuxième Guerre Mondiale, la République française a su rester stable en France, et l’ensemble des Français s’y reconnaissent. Les symboles qui s’y rattachent ont dépassé les frontières du pays, et l’hymne national, comme le drapeau tricolore ou encore Marianne (sans doute grâce au tableau mondialement célèbre de Delacroix), font sans doute partie des symboles les plus facilement reconnaissables dans le monde.
