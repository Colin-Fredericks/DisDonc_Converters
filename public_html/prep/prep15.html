Aspiration in French: "p," "t," and "k"

These three French consonants are similar to their English counterparts, but not identical. When pronounced in English, all three are followed by a puff of air. This is called "aspiration." To test this, hold a lighted match or a thin piece of paper near your lips and say the following words:

The puff of air after the initial consonant of these words should put out the match or cause the piece of paper to move. If the following three French words are pronounced correctly, the match should remain lit or the paper should remain still.

Note that English p, t, and k lose their aspiration or puff of air when they follow s in a word. If you say the words, the lighted match or piece of paper should not be affected, because there is no aspiration.

If you concentrate on how you make the sound, you will see that the tongue touches the inside of the gum ridge above the upper teeth to form English t. La Structure De La Langue 1: Passé Composé Avec “Être”

Some French verbs such as arriver form their passé composé with être as the auxiliary verb, not avoir. When the verb is conjugated with être in the passé composé, it agrees in gender and number with the subject. Listen and repeat.

Je suis arrivé(e) au café.
Nous sommes arrivé(e)s au café.
Tu es arrivé(e) au café.
Vous êtes arrivé(e)(s) au café.
Il est arrivé au café.
Ils sont arrivés au café.
Elle est arrivée au café.
Elles sont arrivées au café.

Letters in parentheses indicate agreeement. The form is determined by the gender and number of people the subject refers to. (For example, when writing to a female friend, you use Je suis arrivée, but when writing to a male friend you use Je suis arrivé.)

Similarly, you write to a female friend Tu es arrivée quand?, but Tu es arrivé quand? to a male friend.

Questions:

  1. Look at the following two forms. When would you use each one?
    1. Nous sommes arrivés.
    2. Nous sommes arrivées.
  2. Look at the following four forms. When would you use each one?
    1. Vous êtes arrivé.
    2. Vous êtes arrivée.
    3. Vous êtes arrivés.
    4. Vous êtes arrivées.
Show Answers
    1. Nous sommes arrivés is used to refer to two or more males or to a group of two or more consisting of males and females.
    2. Nous sommes arrivées refers to two or more females.
    1. Vous êtes arrivé is used in reference to one male that you address as vous.
    2. Vous êtes arrivée is used in reference to one female that you address as vous.
    3. Vous êtes arrivés is used in reference to two or more males or to a group of two or more consisting of males and females.
    4. Vous êtes arrivées isused in reference to two or more females.

Note that these agreements are not heard in speech but are required when writing. The following verbs also form the passé composé with être.

aller: je suis allé(e)

retourner: je suis retourné(e)
revenir: je suis revenu(e)

These verbs have several features in common:

They express motion (except for rester, which expresses lack of motion)

La Structure De La Langue 2: L’Objet Direct

Direct objects are verbal complements that complete the action of the verb. A sentence typically consists of a subject, a verb, and one or more complements. The subject is the element that determines the ending of the verb. Examine the following sentences.

1.
Je vois le professeur.
I see the teacher.
2.
Le professeur écrit au tableau.
The teacher is writing on the board.
3.
Je regarde les mots.
I look at the words.
4.
J’écris les mots dans mon cahier.
I write the words in my notebook.
5.
La cloche sonne.
The bell rings.
6.
Nous sortons de la salle de classe.
We leave the classroom.

Questions:

  1. What are the subjects of the above sentences?
  2. What are the verbs of the above sentences? How do their endings agree with the subject?
  3. What are the complements of the verbs of the above sentences? Which sentences have more than one complement? Which sentence has no complement?
  4. In which of the sentences are there direct objects?
Show Answers
  1. The subjects are bolded in the following responses.
    1. Je vois le professeur.
    2. Le professeur écrit au tableau.
    3. Je regarde les mots.
    4. J’écris les mots dans mon cahier.
    5. La cloche sonne.
    6. Nous sortons de la salle de classe.
  2. The verbs are bolded in the following list. After each sentence the subject and the verb-ending are given in parentheses.
    1. Je vois le professeur. (je: vois)
    2. Le professeur écrit au tableau. (le professeur: écrit)
    3. Je regarde les mots. (je: regarde)
    4. J’écris les mots dans mon cahier. (je: écris)
    5. La cloche sonne. (la cloche: sonne)
    6. Nous sortons de la salle de classe. (nous: sortons)
  3. The complements of the verbs are bolded in the following list.
    1. Je vois le professeur.
    2. Le professeur écrit au tableau.
    3. Je regarde les mots.
    4. J’écris les mots dans mon cahier. The verb écris in this sentence has two complements: les mots and dans mon cahier.
    5. La cloche sonne. The verb sonne in this sentence has no complement.
    6. Nous sortons de la salle de classe.
  4. The direct objects are bolded in the sentences below.
    1. Je vois le professeur.
    2. Le professeur écrit au tableau.
    3. Je regarde les mots.
    4. J’écris les mots dans mon cahier.
    5. La cloche sonne. The verb sonne in this sentence has no complement.
    6. Nous sortons de la salle de classe.

In sentence 1, le professeur is the direct object of the verb vois. In sentence 3, les mots is the direct object of the verb regarde. In sentence 4, les mots is the direct object of the verb écris. In sentence 5, there is no direct object. In sentence 6, de la classe is the direct object of the verb sortons.

Direct objects are complements that stand on their own. They do not require prepositions and they are used to complete the meaning of the verb. (French: complément d’objet direct).

Notice that French and English don’t necessarily use the same type of complement. The verb regarder takes a direct object in French (Je regarde les mots.) but a complement is introduced by the preposition atin English (I look at the words.) Here is a list of verbs that take direct objects in French, but not in English. Remember these abbreviations: qqn (quelqu’un) stands for any person; qqch (quelque chose) represents any noun referring to things.

attendre qqn, attendre qqch

chercher qqn, chercher qqch
to look for someone, to look for something

to listen to someone, to listen to something

Summary of the Direct Object Pronouns “le,” “la,”and “les”

In French, a direct object noun is a noun complement that is joined to its verb without a preposition.

Je vois Thérèse .
I see Thérèse .
Je vois la sortie .
I see the exit .

Direct object nouns in French can be replaced by direct object pronouns.

All masculine singular nouns, whether they refer to people or things, are replaced by le.

Éliane cherche Émile .
Éliane le cherche.
Éliane is looking for him .
Éliane cherche le café .
Éliane le cherche.
Éliane is looking for it .

All feminine singular nouns, whether they refer to people or things, are replaced by la.

Mme Jolivet connaît Éliane.
Mme Jolivet la connaît.
Mme Jolivet knows her.
Mme Jolivet connaît la ville .
Mme Jolivet la connaît.
Mme Jolivet knows it .

All plural direct objects are replaced by les.

Je comprends mes amis.
Je les comprends.
I understand them .
Je comprends ces mots.
Je les comprends.
I understand them .

Position of Object Pronouns

Object pronouns precede the verb in French.

Éliane le cherche.
Mme Jolivet la connaît.
Je les comprends.

The object pronouns le and la become l’ before a vowel.

Ce journal? Je l’ achète tous les jours.
Cette chambre? Je l’ aménage.

When you have verb + infinitive constructions or verb + preposition + infinitive constructions, the object pronoun precedes the infinitive.

Où est le roman? Je veux le lire.
Tu a besoin de cette liste? Tu peux la prendre.
Ces melons sont très bons. Je vais les acheter.
Ce livre est difficile mais je commence à le comprendre.
Mon vélo est vieux. J’ai décidé de la vendre.
Ces exercices sont impossibles. Nous essayons de les finir.

Note that the prepositions à and de do not form contractions with the object pronouns. The object pronouns can also come before the words voilà and voici.

Où est Jean-Claude?
Where is Jean-Claude?
Le voilà, debout au bar.

La Structure De La Langue 4: Les Pronoms Personnels Dans Les Phrases Négatives

In negative sentences, the particle ne precedes the object pronoun.

Tu comprends ce problème?
Do you understand this problem?
Non, je ne le comprends pas du tout.
No, I don’t understand it at all.

Est-ce qu’elle vend les fichiers?
Is she selling the files?
Non, elle ne les vend pas.
No, she’s not selling them.

In verb + infinitive constructions or verb + preposition + infinitive constructions, the negative particles ne and pas are placed around the first verb and the object pronoun precedes the infinitive.

Ce livre est trop long. Je ne vais pas le finir aujourd’hui.
This book is too long. I am not going to finish it today.
Mais tu n’essaies même pas de le finir. Voilà le problème.
But you’re not trying to finish it. That’s the problem.

The imperative is the form of the verb used to tell someone to do something or not to do something. Imperative forms of the verb are sometimes called “command forms.” In English, the imperative is formed by dropping the subject pronoun.

Statement
Command
You take the subway.
Take the subway.
You go to the end of the platform.
Go to the end of the platform.

French forms the imperative in the same way for both the tu and vous forms.

Prenez le métro.
Take the subway .

Take the subway .

In spoken French, almost all imperative forms are the same as the present tense form of the verb minus the subject pronoun. In written French, -er verbs, including aller, drop the final s of the tu form in the imperative.

Note Culturelle: Charles de Gaulle

The article below was written in 1999 as part of the original textbook. It has been left in its original form.

Charles de Gaulle was born in Lille in 1890. At the age of 20, he entered a military school in Saint-Cyr and a few years later served as a junior officer in World War I. After the war, he worked in the Ministry of War and became the personal secretary to Minister Marshal Pétain. When World War II broke out, de Gaulle was named colonel and given the provisional title of general. After the government agreed to an armistice with Germany, de Gaulle voiced his disapproval, insisting than an armistice would humiliate France.

De Gaulle then fled to England, where he delivered what is known as "the appeal of June 18, 1940." By radio, de Gaulle asked that all French citizens refuse to subjugate themselves to the Germans and Pétain and join him in England to fight on the side of the Allies. This act, which today seems very patriotic, was at that time deemed little more than an act of rebellion, worthy of an adventurous man. Very few people joined de Gaulle initially. Little by little, however, with the help of the English, de Gaulle made himself known as the head of the resistance.

When North Africa was seized by the Allies, de Gaulle installed a government in Algeria and prepared to take power after the liberation of France. After Paris was liberated in August 1944, de Gaulle installed and acted as head of a provisional government in the French Republic. The new constitution that was instituted, however, displeased de Gaulle and he resigned, retiring to his house in Colombey-les-deux-églises, in Lorraine, where he wrote his famous account of World War II: Mémoires de guerre.

In 1958 de Gaulle resurfaced on the political scene to assist France during the Algerian war. He agreed to do so on the condition that he be given the power to change the Constitution. De Gaulle then put into place the regime of the "Fifth Republic"and instituted a policy of universal suffrage, which gave all French citizens the opportunity to vote. De Gaulle was elected the first president of France in 1958. His popularity deteriorated, however, when the student movements of 1968 escalated, and he retired in 1969.

In his later years, de Gaulle's political beliefs came under attack. His brand of nationalism was deemed old-fashioned and inappropriate in the modern world. His visionary views of politics stood in stark contrast to the type of nationalism he espoused. This is why there are a number of debates on the political orientation of de Gaulle. What is certain is that de Gaulle was one of the most influential men of his time.
