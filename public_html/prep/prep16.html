French "r"

The sound represented by the letter r in French bears no resemblance to the r sound represented by the same letter in English. French r is formed either by vibrating the uvula (the fleshy appendage that hangs down from the soft palate at the rear of the mouth) or by forcing air between the back of the tongue and the soft palate.

To begin pronouncing French r, we will use words in which r appears after the kand g sounds. French k and g are consonants for which the tongue is in the closest position for pronouncing the r sound.

La Structure De La Langue 1: Le Verbe “Recevoir”

The verb recevoir, to receive or get, is irregular. In the present tense it has a variable stem, and resembles devoir.

Écoutez.
Je reçoisbeaucoup de lettres. Nous recevons beaucoup de lettres.
Tu reçois beaucoup de lettres. Vous recevez beaucoup de lettres.
Il/Elle/On reçoit beaucoup de lettres. Ils/Elles reçoivent beaucoup de lettres.

Note the use of ç to indicate that the letter c represents the s sound before the letter o of the forms reçois, reçoit, and reçoivent.

The past participle of recevoir is reçu. Here is the passé composé of recevoir.

Écoutez.
J’ai reçu un message d’Émile. Nous avons reçu un message d’Émile.
Tu as reçu un message d’Émile. Vous avez reçu un message d’Émile.
Il/Elle/On a reçu un message d’Émile. Ils/Elles ont reçu un message d’Émile.

La Structure De La Langue 2: Le Verbe “Suivre”

Suivre means to follow. Its stem ends in v. The final v of the stem drops in the singular.

Écoutez.
Je suis la voiture rouge. Nous suivons la voiture rouge.
Tu suis la voiture rouge. Vous suivez la voiture rouge.
Il/Elle/On suit la voiture rouge. Ils/Elles suiventla voiture rouge.

Suivre can mean to follow as in the above examples, or to follow the reasoning of as in Éliane’s sentence in the Conversation: Je ne vous suis pas très bien,I don’t follow you (what you say) very well. You must have noticed that je suis, I am, is identical in sound and spelling to je suis, I follow. Context always shows which one is meant. There are ambiguities like this in English too. I put can be either present or past, and the verb read as in I read can be pronounced in two ways, depending on whether you are referring to the past or the present.

The past participle of suivre is suivi.

Écoutez.
Je ne l’ai pas suivi. Nous ne l’avons pas suivi.
Tu ne l’as pas suivi. Vous ne l’avez pas suivi.
Il/Elle/On ne l’a pas suivi. Ils/Elles ne l’ont pas suivi.

Suivre is used in the common expression suivre un cours, to take a course.

La Structure De La Langue 3: Le Verbe “Craindre”

Craindre means to fear. Its stem ends in gn. The final gn of the stem drops in the singular.

Écoutez.
Je crains que oui. Nous craignons que oui.
Tu crains que oui. Vous craignez que oui.
Il/Elle/On craint que oui. Ils/Elles craignent que oui.

Craindre is more formal in tone than avoir peur, much the same way that the verb to fear is more formal than to be afraid in English. The past participle of craindre is craint.

Écoutez.
J’ai craint que non. Nous avons craint que non.
Tu as craint que non. Vous avez craint que non.
Il/Elle/On a craint que non. Ils/Elles ont craint que non.

La Structure De La Langue 4: Le Verbe “Offrir”

The verb offrir means to offer and to give as a gift. Although offrir has an infinitive that ends in ir, it is conjugated in the present tense like an -er verb, the way ouvrir and découvrir are.

Here is the present tense of offrir:

Écoutez.
Je lui offre le stage. Nous lui offrons le stage.
Tu lui offres le stage. Vous lui offrez le stage.
Il/Elle/On lui offre le stage. Ils/Elles lui offrent le stage.

Like ouvrir and découvrir,the verb offrirhas a past participle that ends in ert. Here is the passé composé of offrir:

Écoutez.
Je leur ai offert une bonne situation. Nous leur avons offert une bonne situation.
Tu leur as offert une bonne situation. Vous leur avez offert une bonne situation.
Il/Elle/On leur a offert une bonne situation. Ils/Elles leur ont offert une bonne situation.

La Structure De La Langue 5: L’Objet Indirect

We have seen that direct objects are complements (nouns or pronouns) joined directly to the verb (without a preposition).

The words in boldface are direct objects.

Je retrouve mes copains.

Tu connais Paris?

On regarde le magazine.

Nous écoutons nos CDs.

Ils aiment le rock.

Now look at the following sentence.

J’écris une lettre à mes parents.

In the above sentence, une lettre is the direct object, but the sentence has an additional complement, à mes parents. The noun phrase mes parents is not joined directly to the verb, but is linked to it by the preposition à. It is therefore called an “indirect object” (in French, complément d’objet indirect).

An indirect object in French is an animate noun (the French indirect object must refer to people or animals) joined to the verb by the preposition à.

The English indirect object may be preceded by the preposition to or appear without a preposition before the direct object. Compare the following two sentences.

I’m writing a letter to my parents.

I’m writing my parents a letter.

In both sentences, my parents, is the indirect object. English also allows inanimate nouns (nouns referring to things) to fill the indirect object position in sentences.

We gave the house a good cleaning.

Note that indirect objects referring to people usually occur together with a direct object referring to an object, as in the following sentence where une lettre is the direct object (referring to an inanimate object) and mes parentsis the indirect object (referring to people).

J’écris une lettre à mes parents.

Answers

It is important to remember that as an indirect object pronoun, lui can refer to males or females, since as a stressed pronoun, lui refers only to il.

Here are some of the most common verbs that appear in the construction verb + direct object of the thing + indirect object of the person. The model sentences are recorded for you to listen and repeat. In the listing of the expressions, qqch is the abbreviation for quelque chose and represents any inanimate noun; qqn is the abbreviation for quelqu’un and represents any animate noun. Note that some of the indirect objects in English use the preposition for instead of to.

Écoutez.
apporter qqch à qqn to bring something to someone Le chien apporte la balle à Baudoin.
donner qqch à qqn to give something to someone Le client donne l’argent à la vendeuse.
dire qqch à qqn to say something to someone, to tell someone something J’ai dit à mes amis que j’ai peur.
envoyer qqch à qqn to send something to someone On a envoyé une lettre aux employés.
expliquer qqch à qqn to explain something to someone La mère de Baudoin explique les problèmes de maths à ses étudiants.
laisser qqch à qqn to leave something for someone Il faut laisser un message à Éliane.
lire qqch à qqn to read something to someone Tu as lu l’histoire à Baudoin?
louer qqch à qqn to rent something to someone Éliane loue une chambre à Régine.
montrer qqch à qqn to show something to someone Éliane montre les fichiers à ses amis.
offrir qqch à qqn to give something to someone (as a gift) M. et Mme Jolivet ont offert un train électrique à Baudoin.
prêter qqch à qqn to lend something to someone Je ne prête pas ma voiture à mes amis.
proposer qqch à qqn to suggest, propose something to someone Ils proposent un stage en Suisse aux nouveaux employés.
rendre qqch à qqn to give something to someone Tu as rendu l’argent à Michel?
vendre qqch à qqn to sell something to someone Nous avons vendu nos meubles à nos voisins.

Note that with demander, which has the same construction in French as the above verbs, the English translation does not parallel the French.

Écoutez.
demander qqch à qqn to ask someone for something Éliane a demandé une explication à Émile.

The French sentence has an inanimate direct object (une explication) and an animate indirect object (à Émile).

Note that a few verbs can appear with an indirect object, but with no direct object.

Écoutez.
parler à qqn to speak to someone J’ai besoin de parler au PDG.
répondre à qqn to answer someone Les étudiants répondent au professeur.
téléphoner à qqn to phone someone Je téléphone tous les jours à ma petite amie.

The verb écrire may or may not have a direct object.

Écoutez.
écrire à qqn to write to someone Mes cousins écrivent souvent à mon oncle.
écrire qqch à qqn to write something to someone J’ai écrit une lettre à mes parents ce matin.

The verb demander may appear without a direct object.

Écoutez.
demander à qqn to ask someone Si tu ne comprends pas, tu peux demander au professeur.

The rules for position of the indirect object pronouns lui and leur are the same as for the direct object pronouns.

Object pronouns go before the verb.

Écoutez.

Je veux parler avec Maurice. Je lui téléphone.

In verb + infinitive constructions, the object pronoun is placed before the infinitive.

Écoutez.

Nicole m’a laissé un message. Je dois lui téléphoner.

Object pronouns are placed before the auxiliary verb in the passé composé.

Écoutez.

Mes amis ont voulu sortir. Je leur ai prêté ma voiture.

Note that indirect object pronouns do not cause agreement of the past participle.

What is the position of object pronouns in affirmative commands (imperatives that tell someone to do something)?

What is the position of object pronouns in negative commands (imperatives that tell someone notto do something)?

What happens to the pronoun me in affirmative commands?

Is there any change in the pronoun me in negative commands?

Show Answers

Answers

In affirmative commands, object pronouns follow the verb. In writing, they are attached to the verb with a hyphen.

In negative commands, object pronouns appear in their normal position before the verb.

When placed after the verb in affirmative commands, the pronoun me (whether it is a direct or indirect object) becomes moi.

There is no change in the pronoun me in negative commands.
