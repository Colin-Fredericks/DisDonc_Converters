3. Prononciation

Prononciation

Review and practice the contrast between the French vowels ou and u. The vowel represented by the letters ou in French spelling is a high, back vowel. The tongue is arched toward the upper back of the mouth, almost as if you were going to make the k sound. The lips are rounded and protruding. The position of the tongue is higher and further back than for the vowel in the English word food, and the muscles of the mouth are tenser.

The French vowel u is a high front vowel with no English equivalent. To pronounce it, the tongue is high and front in the mouth (in the same position as for the French vowel represented by i), but the lips are rounded as if pronouncing ou. Use of the future perfect

The future perfect stresses the completion of an action in future time.

Écoutez.

Quand est-ce que tu auras fini l’article?

By when will you have finished the article?

À huit heures nous aurons mangé.

We will have eaten by eight.

The future perfect indicates an action that will be completed in the future before another action takes place. The future perfect is especially common in subordinate clauses after dès que and aussitôt que, as soon as. The main verb in these sentences is in the simple future or imperative.

Écoutez.

Aussitôt qu’ils seront partis nous sortirons.

As soon as they leave, we’ll go out.

Téléphone-nous dès que tu seras arrivé.

Call us as soon as you get there.

Note that English often uses a simple present-tense form in these cases.

The future perfect can express the cause of a future event.

Écoutez.

Ils ne pourront pas vous expliquer ce problème parce qu’ils ne l’auront pas compris.

They won’t be able to explain this problem to you because they won’t have understood it.

Il ne saura pas y arriver parce qu’il n’aura pas demandé son chemin.

He won’t know how to get there because he won’t have asked directions.

La Structure De La Langue 3: L’Emploi Du Plus-Que-Parfait Et Du Passé Du Conditionnel Dans Les Hypothèses

You have seen two types of conditional sentences so far. Conditional sentences that express hypotheses use the future tense in the main clause and the present tense in the si clause. Listen and repeat.

Écoutez.

Si nous nous mettons en route de bonne heure, nous arriverons à l’heure du dîner.

If we set out early, we’ll arrive at dinnertime.

Nous pourrons prendre le téléphérique si nous arrivons avant six heures.

We can take the ski lift if we get there before six o’clock.

When a conditional sentence implements a hypothesis that is contrary to a fact in present time, then the si clause is in the imperfect and the main clause is in the conditional. This construction parallels English usage.

Fact

Contrary-to-fact hypothesis

Écoutez.

Baudoin a des vacances. Il passe la journée au bureau.

Baudoin has a vacation. He spends the day at the office.

Si Baudoin n’avait pas de vacances, il ne passerait pas la journée au bureau.

If Baudoin weren't on vacation, he wouldn’t spend the day at the office.

Fact

Contrary-to-fact hypothesis

Écoutez.

Paul a un téléphone portable. Il peut téléphoner à Solange.

Paul has a portable telephone. He can call Solange.

Si Paul n'avait pas de téléphone portable, il ne pourrait pas téléphoner à Solange.

If Paul didn't have a portable telephone, he wouldn't be able to call Solange.

Now look at the following sentences. Listen and repeat.

Fact

Contrary-to-fact hypothesis

Écoutez.

M. Toësca n’était pas au bureau. On l’a soupçonné d’être un espion industriel.

Mr. Toësca was not at the office. People suspected him of being a spy.

Si M. Toësca avait été au bureau, on ne l’aurait pas soupçonné d’être un espion industriel.

If Mr. Toësca had been at the office, people would not have suspected him of being a spy.

Fact

Contrary-to-fact hypothesis

Écoutez.

Les copains ont pris le téléphérique. L’homme à l’imperméable gris a essayé d’en couper le câble.

The friends took the ski lift. The man in the gray raincoat tried to cut the cable.

Si les copains n’avaient pas pris le téléphérique, l’homme à l’imperméable gris n’aurait pas essayé d’en couper le câble.

If the friends hadn’t taken the ski lift, the man in the gray raincoat wouldn’t have tried to cut the cable.

La Structure De La Langue 4: Le Préfixe "Re"

The prefix re is commonly added to verbs in French to convey the idea of doing the action again.

Ne recommence pas!

Don’t start again!

Sometimes the addition of the prefix re (often r before a vowel) creates a verb with a very different meaning from the root.
