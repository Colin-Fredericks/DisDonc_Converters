LA STRUCTURE DE LA LANGUE 2: CONTRASTE ENTRE LE SUBJONCTIF ET L’INDICATIF APRÈS L’INTERROGATIF

Examine the following sentences. Listen and repeat.

Écoutez.

  

Je ne crois pas que le téléphérique soit encore en service.

I don’t think the ski lift is still running.

Croyez-vous que ce train desserve les villages?

Do you think that this train stops at the villages?

Je ne pense pas qu’on puisse prendre le car postal.

I don’t think we can take the post office bus.

Penses-tu que l’avion soit à l’heure?

Do you think the plane is on time?

   1.          Can you state the meanings shared by the main clauses?

   2.          Which verbs appear in the main clauses? In what forms do these verbs appear?

   3.          What form of the verb is used in the subordinate clauses beginning with que?

ANSWERS

Now examine these sentences. Listen and repeat.

Écoutez.

  

Je crois que le téléphérique est encore en service.

I think the ski lift is still running.

Vous croyez que ce train dessert les villages.

You think that this train stops at the villages.

Je pense qu’on peut prendre le car postal.

I think we can take the post office bus.

Tu penses que l’avion est à l’heure.

You think the plane is on time.

   1.          Which verbs appear in the main clauses? In what forms do these verbs appear?

   2.          What form of the verb is used in the subordinate clauses beginning with que?

ANSWERS

Note that the indicative may be used after the negative or interrogative of croire and penser. The indicative implies that the speaker is fairly sure that the subordinate clause is true.

Je ne crois pas qu’il vienne. / I (really) don’t think he’s coming.

Je ne crois pas qu’il vient. / I don’t think he’s coming (but he may).

LA STRUCTURE DE LA LANGUE 3: LE PARTICIPE PASSÉ POUR EXPRIMER LA POSITION DU CORPS

To express positions of the human body or of objects, English uses the present participle (-ing form) while French uses the past participle. Here are the most common examples.

French

English

assis

sitting

couché

lying down, in bed

levé

up, out of bed

allongé

stretched out, lying down

accoudé

leaning on one’s elbows

courbé

stooping over

penché

leaning out

appuyé

leaning against

Notice, however, that standing is translated in French as debout, which is not a past participle. Debout can also mean up, out of bed.

These past participles are derived from reflexive verbs that mean to assume the position expressed by the verb.

French

English

s’asseoir

to sit down

se coucher

to lie down in bed, go to bed

se lever

to get up

s’allonger

to lie down, stretch out

s’accouder

to lean on one’s elbows

se courber

to stoop over

se pencher

to lean out

s’appuyer (contre/sur)

to lean (against)
