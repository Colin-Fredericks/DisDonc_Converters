Langue et Culture 1

French universities are traditionally divided into facultés, or schools, such as la faculté de droit for law school, la faculté de lettres for humanities, la faculté de médecine for medical school, and la faculté de sciences humaines for social sciences. University students attend only one of these schools, which are not necessarily located in the same part of the city. To be at college is être à la faculté, or à la fac for short.

3. Pronunciation Practice

Pronunciation Practice

Closed "é"

The sound represented by é in French spelling is called the closed é. It sounds similar to the ay of play in English, but there are some important differences between the English and French sounds.

When you make the sound in English, the position of the lips and tongue shift as the sound is made, producing a diphthong: two vowels pronounced in a single syllable. The sound starts out like the eof bed and finishes as the ee of feed. The French closed é is not a diphthong. The tip of the tongue is pointing at the gum ridge behind the upper teeth, while the mouth is slightly more open than for the sound of i as in il, Régine, or suisse. The position of the tongue, mouth, and lips does not change during the production of the sound.

The vowel sound of the closed é in French is short, tense, and uniform. Listen to the following English words containing the diphthong represented by ay, followed by French words containing é. Note that the French sound é can be spelled in several different ways.

La Structure de la Langue 2: Gender

The Indefinite Article

The French indefinite article (English a/an) has two forms: un and une. French nouns are divided into two broad classes: those taking the indefinite article un and those taking the indefinite article une. In traditional terminology, nouns requiring un are called “masculine nouns” and nouns requiring une are called “feminine nouns.” The division of nouns into these two categories is called gender. All nouns in French, whether they refer to people or things, belong to one of these two categories.

Note Culturelle :
The French and the Euro

The euro has been the official currency of France and the other members of the European Union since January 1, 1999. The launch of this new European currency marks the last phase in a process of monetary reconciliation among European countries that began in the mid 1970s after the collapse of the 1944 Bretton Woods Agreement. This agreement was reached in 1944 when several nations gathered in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, to develop structures and institutions to support a revitalization of the worldwide economy in the wake of World War II.

The implementation of the euro as the economic component of the process of European reorganization constitutes the most spectacular test of Europe's solidity to date. Debates that continue to agitate European countries testify to the difficulty of this test. In relinquishing the management of monetary policy to the Banque Centrale Européenne (Bce) based in Frankfurt, Germany, France and other members of the European Union renounced a major tool of economic policy in the hope that increased profits in the future would justify an abandonment of sovereignty.

The euro presents at least three advantages for France. First, a unified currency allows for a better coordination of European monetary policy. Second, the move permits the growth of a singular European marketplace, which increases consumer buying power through scaled economies and increased competition. Finally, the euro acts as a substitute for the dollar in capital markets and, therefore, allows European states to finance themselves at a lower cost.

Despite the advantages, support for the euro, like the European Union in general, is far from unanimous in France. The "yes" brought back from the Treaty of Maastricht referendum in 1992 marked the acceptance by European Union countries to transfer monetary power to the BCE but came with a sense of upheaval. The ongoing controversy sparked by the treaty crosses traditional political frontiers; "pro-Europeans" and "anti-Europeans" can be found in both right-wing and left-wing parties.

The pro-Europeans argue that because France competes with the significant economic power of American and other economic giants emerging in Asia, the use of the euro is the only hope for a bright economic future. They also contend that the European reorganization will force countries to make necessary structural reforms, especially at the institutional level.

On the other hand, the anti-Europeans claim that France risks a dilution of its national identity as knowledge and economic procedures are shared throughout Europe. They also assert that the loss of sovereignty exposes the country to unwanted measures and reforms, particularly those of a social and fiscal nature, resulting from one of the consequences of successful monetary unification - the mobility of labor to compensate eventual imbalances in different regions.

What is certain for the moment is that the "euro zone" of Europe is already heterogeneous in terms of wealth and beliefs and that no unified monetary policy will be able to satisfy everyone. However, because France has been at the heart of the unification process from the start, it knows how to be heard, particularly when French interests are at stake. For example, at the insistence of the French government, members of the European Monetary Union were persuaded to name the former president of the Bank of France as the current vice-president of the Bce.
