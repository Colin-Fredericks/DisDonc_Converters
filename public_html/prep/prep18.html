La Structure De La Langue 1: Le Verbe “Mentir”

The verb mentir, to lie (i.e., tell a falsehood), is conjugated like partir and servir. The final consonant of the stem, t, is not heard in the third-person singular.

Je ne mens pas.
Nous ne mentons pas.
Tu ne mens pas.
Vous ne mentez pas.
Il/Elle/On ne ment pas.
Ils/Elles ne mentent pas.

Mentir forms the passé composé with avoir.

Je n’ai pas menti.
Nous n’avons pas menti.
Tu n’as pas menti.
Vous n’avez pas menti.
Il/Elle/On n’a pas menti.
Ils/Elles n’ont pas menti.

The imperfect of mentir is formed regularly.

Je ne mentais pas.
Nous ne mentions pas.
Tu ne mentais pas.
Vous ne mentiez pas.
Il/Elle/On ne mentait pas.
Il/Elles ne mentaient pas.

La Structure De La Langue 3: L’Inversion Dans L’Interrogation

So far you have learned two ways to ask questions in French.

By changing the intonation of the sentence from falling to rising without changing the order of elements

Questions formed solely by a change of intonation are typical of informal spoken French.

Vous avez vu les fichiers?
Have you seen the files?
Ils ont fini leur interrogation?
Have they finished their questioning?

In this method of question formation, question words are placed at the end of the sentence.

On l’emmène où?
Where are they taking him?
Il se méfiait de qui?
Who was he mistrustful of?

By adding est-ce que to the beginning of the statement or right after the question word

These questions are acceptable at all levels of speech.

Est-ce que vous avez vu les fichiers?
Have you seen the files?
Est-ce qu’ils ont fini leur interrogation?
Have they finished their questioning?
Où est-ce qu’on l’emmène?
Where are they taking him?
De qui est-ce qu’il se méfiait?
Who was he mistrustful of?

Now listen to the following questions:

Avez-vous vu les fichiers?
Have you seen the files?
Ont-ils fini leur interrogation?
Have they finished their questioning?
De qui se méfiait-il?
Who was he mistrustful of?

Questions:

  1. Why does the placement of the subject pronoun in the above questions indicate a question and not a statement?
  2. How is the pronoun connected to the verb in written French?
Show Answers
  1. The subject pronoun is placed after the verb to indicate that a question, not a statement, is being formulated. This procedure is called inversion and the questions formed by placing the subject pronoun after the verb are called inverted questions.
  2. In written French, the subject pronoun is attached to the verb by a hyphen. Inversion takes place only with pronouns, not with nouns.

Consider the following questions:

Où l’emmène-t-on?
Where are they taking him?
Quand va-t-il au travail?
When does he go to work?
Ajoute-t-elle des précisions?
Is she adding details?
A-t-il besoin d’un taxi?
Does he need a taxi?
  1. Do the above verb forms end in consonants or vowels?
  2. What is added between the verb and the pronoun?
Show Answers
  1. The verb forms end in a vowel.
  2. When a verb form ends in a vowel and the subject is placed after the verb to form an inverted question, t is added before il, elle, and on.

Now study the inverted questions in the negative.

Ne comprennent-ils pas?
Don’t they understand?

Doesn’t she work there anymore?

Note that the negative phrases ne... pas and ne... plus surround the inverted subject and verb.

Now look at inverted questions in the passé composé.

Avez-vous fini?
Did you finish?
Combien de temps ont-ils attendu?
How long did they wait?
Quand est-elle arrivée?
When did she arrive?
Pourquoi ne sont-ils pas restés?

Questions:

  1. Which verb is inverted with the subject pronoun - the past participle or the auxiliary?
  2. Where are the negative words placed?
Show Answers
  1. The auxiliary verb (either avoir or être) is inverted with the subject pronoun.
  2. The negative words are placed around the inverted subject and auxiliary verb: Pourquoi ne sont-ils pas restés?

Use of Inverted Questions

Inverted questions are not used much in everyday speech. They are more typical of formal language and are therefore found in formal writing and formal speech. Thus, questions such as Aménages-tu ta chambre? are much less frequent than Aménagez-vous votre chambre? In the Conversation of this lesson, Éliane quotes the policemen’s questions during the interrogation.

  • Êtes-vous sûre, Mademoiselle?
  • N’avez-vous plus rien à ajouter, Mademoiselle?
  • Pouvez-vous nous donner encore des précisions, Mademoiselle?

An interrogation is a situation in which formal language is called for, and, therefore, during which inverted questions might be used.

La Structure De La Langue 4: Le Discours Indirect

Les questions oui/non et les déclarations

There are two ways to tell someone what other people have said.

You can quote their words directly.

Original question by the policeman: Êtes-vous sûre, Mademoiselle?

L’agent de police a demandé à Éliane: «Êtes-vous sûre, Mademoiselle?»
The policeman asked Éliane, “Are you sure, Miss?”

You can quote their words indirectly (in other words, report their words) by incorporating them as part of a subordinate clause.

L’agent de police a demandé à Éliane si elle était sûre.
The policeman asked Éliane if she was sure.

Now look at the French sentences again.

  1. L’agent de police a demandé à Éliane: «Êtes-vous sûre, Mademoiselle?»
  2. L’agent de police a demandé à Éliane si elle était sûre.

Questions:

  1. In what tense was the directly quoted question asked?
  2. In what tense was the indirectly quoted question reported?
  3. How does the person or subject of the verb change in reported questions?
Show Answers
  1. In sentence A, the policeman asks Éliane, “Êtes-vous sûre, Mademoiselle?” This directly quoted question is in the present tense.
  2. In sentence B, the indirectly quoted question is reported in the imperfect tense. Questions originally asked in the present are usually reported in the imperfect tense.
  3. Questions asked to tu or vous are reported in the third person: il, elle, ils, elles. The reference to the third person appears in the quoting of the direct question.

J’ai demandé à Éliane: «Tu es fatiguée?»
I asked Éliane, “Are you tired?”

The appearance of Éliane in the direct question shows what pronoun will be used when reporting the question.

J’ai demandé à Éliane si elle était fatiguée.
I asked Éliane whether she was tired.

The syntax is the same in both French and English.

Note that the first person can also replace tu and vous in reported speech.

When inversion is used to ask questions, qu’est-ce que is replaced by que. Que becomes qu’ before a verb form beginning with a vowel.

Qu’achetez-vous?

Qu’ont-ils vu?

Note that these elided forms are extremely rare in speech. They are limited to formal, written French.

La Structure De La Langue 7: Les Verbes Pronominaux

Many verbs in French appear with an object pronoun that refers to the same person as the subject of the sentence. This pronoun is called a “reflexive pronoun.” Because of the mandatory reflexive pronoun, these verbs are called “pronominal verbs.” Most pronominal verbs in French correspond to intransitive verbs (verbs that do not have a direct object) in English. Examine the present tense of se reposer, to rest.

Je me repose.
Nous nous reposons.
Tu te reposes.
Vous vous reposez.
Il/Elle/On se repose.
Ils/Elles se reposent.

Questions:

  1. How do the reflexive pronouns used with je, tu, nous, and vous compare with the direct and indirect object pronouns for those persons?
  2. What is the reflexive pronoun for il, elle, and on?
  3. What is the reflexive pronoun for ils and elles?
  4. Does the third-person reflexive pronoun change to show gender and number?
Show Answers
  1. The reflexive pronouns me, te, nous, and vous are the same as the corresponding direct and indirect object pronouns. What makes them reflexive is that they refer to the same person as the subject of the verb. For instance, in Tu te reposes, tu and te refer to the same person, the person you are talking to.
  2. The reflexive pronoun for il, elle, and on is se>. Remember that On se repose can mean either people rest or we rest.
  3. The reflexive pronoun for ils and elles is se>.
  4. No, the reflexive pronoun is se for all third-person subjects, masculine and feminine, singular and plural.

Now examine the forms of se reposer in the passé composé.

Je me suis reposé(e) .
Nous nous sommes reposé(e)s.
Tu t’es reposé(e).
Vous vous êtes reposé(e)(s).
Il s’est reposé.
Ils se sont reposés.
Elle s’est reposée.
Elles se sont reposées.
On s’est reposé(e)(s).

Note that the past participle agrees with the reflexive pronoun when the reflexive pronoun is the direct object of the verb. This is the case in most reflexive verbs.

Reflexive pronouns follow the same rules of position as object pronouns. In verb + infinitive constructions, they precede the infinitive. Note that the reflexive pronoun changes, even when accompanied by the infinitive, to agree with the subject.

Je veux me reposer.
I want to rest.

Tu dois te méfier de lui.
You ought to beware of him.
Vous ne voudriez pas vous tromper.
You wouldn’t want to make a mistake.
