La Structure De La Langue 1: Le Verbe “Mentir”

The verb mentir, to lie (i.e., tell a falsehood), is conjugated like partir and servir. The final consonant of the stem, t, is not heard in the third-person singular.

Je ne mens pas.

Nous ne mentons pas.

Tu ne mens pas.

Vous ne mentez pas.

Il/Elle/On ne ment pas.

Ils/Elles ne mentent pas.

Mentir forms the passé composé with avoir.

Je n’ai pas menti.

Nous n’avons pas menti.

Tu n’as pas menti.

Vous n’avez pas menti.

Il/Elle/On n’a pas menti.

Ils/Elles n’ont pas menti.

The imperfect of mentir is formed regularly.

Je ne mentais pas.

Nous ne mentions pas.

Tu ne mentais pas.

Vous ne mentiez pas.

Il/Elle/On ne mentait pas.

Il/Elles ne mentaient pas.

La Structure De La Langue 3: L’Inversion Dans L’Interrogation

So far you have learned two ways to ask questions in French.

By changing the intonation of the sentence from falling to rising without changing the order of elements

Questions formed solely by a change of intonation are typical of informal spoken French.

Vous avez vu les fichiers?

Have you seen the files?

Ils ont fini leur interrogation?

Have they finished their questioning?

In this method of question formation, question words are placed at the end of the sentence.

On l’emmène où?

Where are they taking him?

Il se méfiait de qui?

Who was he mistrustful of?

By adding est-ce que to the beginning of the statement or right after the question word

These questions are acceptable at all levels of speech.

Est-ce que vous avez vu les fichiers?

Have you seen the files?

Est-ce qu’ils ont fini leur interrogation?

Have they finished their questioning?

Où est-ce qu’on l’emmène?

Where are they taking him?

De qui est-ce qu’il se méfiait?

Who was he mistrustful of?

Use of Inverted Questions

Inverted questions are not used much in everyday speech. They are more typical of formal language and are therefore found in formal writing and formal speech. Thus, questions such as Aménages-tu ta chambre? are much less frequent than Aménagez-vous votre chambre? In the Conversation of this lesson, Éliane quotes the policemen’s questions during the interrogation.

Êtes-vous sûre, Mademoiselle?

N’avez-vous plus rien à ajouter, Mademoiselle?

Pouvez-vous nous donner encore des précisions, Mademoiselle?

An interrogation is a situation in which formal language is called for, and, therefore, during which inverted questions might be used.

La Structure De La Langue 4: Le Discours Indirect

Les questions oui/non et les déclarations

There are two ways to tell someone what other people have said.

You can quote their words directly.

Original question by the policeman: Êtes-vous sûre, Mademoiselle?

L’agent de police a demandé à Éliane: «Êtes-vous sûre, Mademoiselle?»

The policeman asked Éliane, “Are you sure, Miss?”

You can quote their words indirectly (in other words, report their words) by incorporating them as part of a subordinate clause.

L’agent de police a demandé à Éliane si elle était sûre.

The policeman asked Éliane if she was sure.

Now look at the French sentences again.

L’agent de police a demandé à Éliane: «Êtes-vous sûre, Mademoiselle?»

L’agent de police a demandé à Éliane si elle était sûre.

In what tense was the directly quoted question asked?

In what tense was the indirectly quoted question reported?

How does the person or subject of the verb change in reported questions?

J’ai demandé à Éliane: «Tu es fatiguée?»

I asked Éliane, “Are you tired?”

The appearance of Éliane in the direct question shows what pronoun will be used when reporting the question.

J’ai demandé à Éliane si elle était fatiguée.

I asked Éliane whether she was tired.

The syntax is the same in both French and English.

Note that the first person can also replace tu and vous in reported speech.

Éliane m’a demandé: «Tu veux manger?»

Éliane asked me, “Do you want to eat?”

Éliane m’a demandé si je voulais manger.

Éliane asked me if I wanted to eat.

Éliane nous a demandé: «Vous voulez manger?»

Éliane asked us, “Do you want to eat?”

Éliane nous a demandé si nous voulions manger.

Éliane asked us if we wanted to eat.

How is what expressed when inversion is used to form a question?

Answer

Qu’achetez-vous?

Note that these elided forms are extremely rare in speech. They are limited to formal, written French.

When questions beginningwith qu’est-ce qui, qu’est-ce que, and que are incorporated into larger statements as reported speech, they are replaced by ce qui and ce que.

Ce qui replaces qu’est-ce qui while ce que replaces qu’est-ce que and que:

Qu’est-ce qui t’embête?

What’s annoying you?

Je voudrais savoir ce qui t’embête.

Qu’est-ce que vous dites?

What are you saying?

Je ne comprends pas ce que vous dites.

I don’t understand what you are saying.

Que prend-il?

What is he having?

Dites-moi ce qu’il prend.

Tell me what he’s having.

La Structure De La Langue 7: Les Verbes Pronominaux

Many verbs in French appear with an object pronoun that refers to the same person as the subject of the sentence. This pronoun is called a “reflexive pronoun.” Because of the mandatory reflexive pronoun, these verbs are called “pronominal verbs.” Most pronominal verbs in French correspond to intransitive verbs (verbs that do not have a direct object) in English. Examine the present tense of se reposer, to rest.

Je me repose.

Nous nous reposons.

Tu te reposes.

Vous vous reposez.

Il/Elle/On se repose.

Ils/Elles se reposent.

How do the reflexive pronouns used with je, tu, nous, and vous compare with the direct and indirect object pronouns for those persons?

What is the reflexive pronoun for il, elle, and on?

What is the reflexive pronoun for ils and elles?

Does the third-person reflexive pronoun change to show gender and number?

Now examine the forms of se reposer in the passé composé.

Je me suis reposé(e).

Nous nous sommes reposé(e)s.

Tu t’es reposé(e).

Vous vous êtes reposé(e)(s).

Il s’est reposé.

Ils se sont reposés.

Elle s’est reposée.

Elles se sont reposées.

On s’est reposé(e)(s).

Note that the past participle agrees with the reflexive pronoun when the reflexive pronoun is the direct object of the verb. This is the case in most reflexive verbs.

Reflexive pronouns follow the same rules of position as object pronouns. In verb + infinitive constructions, they precede the infinitive. Note that the reflexive pronoun changes, even when accompanied by the infinitive, to agree with the subject.

Je veux me reposer.

I want to rest.

Ils vont s’ennuyer dans cette ville.

They are going to be bored in that city.
