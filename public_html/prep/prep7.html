Français Écrit / Français Parlé 1

The third category of adjectives, adjectives that have two spoken forms and four written forms, can be analyzed in two ways. In written French, these adjectives add an e to the masculine singular to form the feminine singular (patient/patiente). These adjectives then add s to form the plural to each of the singular forms. However, in spoken French it is useful to consider the feminine as the base form. You derive the masculine form from the feminine by deleting the final pronounced consonant. Thus, patiente, a feminine form, is pronounced with a -t sound at the end of the word. To form the masculine, delete the final t sound (spelled patient).

Many adjectives that have irregular spellings in written French are regular in spoken French. In other words, they work by deleting the final pronounced consonant.

In spoken French, adjectives ending in eux in the masculine and euse in the feminine end in the z sound in the feminine. The final z sound is deleted to form the masculine.

Ma sœur est heureuse.
My sister is happy.
Mon frère est heureux.
My brother is happy.

Note that if the masculine singular of an adjective ends in x or s, no additional s is added for the masculine plural. To form the feminine plural, however, you must add s to the feminine singular:

La Structure de la Langue 2

Intensification of Adjectives (1)

Adjectives modify nouns. Words that modify verbs or adjectives are called adverbs. The most common adverb used to intensify an adjective is très:

This house is very small.

Très can be doubled for additional emphasis:

This man is very, very intelligent.

The word bien serves the same function as très before an adjective.

Uncle Robert is very generous.

The word vraiment can be used before adjectives.

Uncle Robert is really generous.

The adverbs tellement and drôlement can replace très. They give the sentence a colloquial ring.

I’m so tired, very tired.
She’s very jealous.

The adverbs complètement and absolument can also be used to emphasize the quality of the adjective. They don’t make sense before all adjectives, however.

I’m completely stressed out.
It’s an absolutely thrilling novel.

Intensification of Adjectives (2)

The adverb assez, rather, intensifies the adjective to a lesser degree than très.

Je suis assez stressée.
Il est assez sympa.

The phrase un peu intensifies the adjective to a lesser degree.

This book is somewhat boring.
This suitcase is a bit heavy.

For too much of a quality, use the adverb trop:

That boy is too shy.
This restaurant is too expensive.

Intensification of Adjectives (3): Antonyms

Different words are sometimes used to express the opposite of an adjective:

mince
thin
gros
fat
intelligent
intelligent
bête
stupid

Some adjectives take a negative prefix to express the opposite quality:

patient
patient
impatient
impatient
possible
possible
impossible
impossible

The word peu, little, can often be used to form the opposite of adjectives. Don’t confuse peu with un peu, a little bit:

He’s impatient.
This course is uninteresting.

The adverb très can intensify peu:

He’s very impatient.

To stress the absence of a quality the negative of a verb can be used with pas du tout, not at all:

He’s not patient at all.

The phrase du toutcan also come at the end of the sentence:

He’s not patient at all.

Note Culturelle :
Napoleon's Influence on Stendhal and His Times

The life of French writer Stendhal allows us to understand the influence of the Napoleonic myth on an entire generation of French youth in the first years of the nineteenth century. Stendhal (Henri Beyle was his birth name) was born in Grenoble in 1783, a few years before the French Revolution broke out in 1789. Instead of pursuing his studies, he joined Napoleon's army and became thoroughly involved in the Napoleonic adventure across Europe. After Napoleon's fall, he returned to France only to find himself scorned and impoverished, as did all of Napoleon's former followers, plunged into disgrace in the wake of the new Restoration regime. It was during this period that Henri Beyle devoted himself entirely to literature under the pseudonym of Stendhal. His first great novel, The Red and the Black (Le Rouge et le Noir), was published in 1830. His second, The Charterhouse of Parma (La Chartreuse de Parme), was published in 1839. In 1842, he fell gravely ill and died in Paris. Stendhal's fascination with Napoleon had inspired him to an exceptional destiny, and he devoted his life to the search for happiness. But, just like his hero Julien Sorel in The Red and the Black who ends up on the guillotine, Stendhal never really found that happiness and Napoleon's fall in 1815 was for him a true tragedy.

If Napoleon influenced the youth and the mentality of his time to such a great extent, it was because he did not owe his success to his ancestry or family name, and this was unheard of in his time. Born in 1769 into a Corsican family of rather modest means, this minor corporal became known for his military victories in Italy and even Egypt, as well as for his reputedly remarkable intelligence. He was so close to power only a few years later that he seized it in 1799 through a coup d'état. He became consul, and then reigned as emperor until 1815. His reign was marked by permanent war against other European countries, which considered him a threat against monarchy. Indeed, he invaded most of Europe, bringing the new ideas of the French Revolution and overthrowing the old absolute monarchies. His ideas were especially appealing to the youth of the day that, like Stendhal, saw in Napoleon a Romantic hero fighting for liberty and progress. Nevertheless, it is important to recognize that Napoleon's image was two-sided: on one hand, he appeared as the young Romantic hero overthrowing monarchies and leading a revolutionary cause, and on the other hand he gave the impression of an orderly man who restored a semblance of dynastic monarchy to France. His designation as emperor and his attempts to promote his own family to political positions make him, if not a king, a kind of new Caesar who established a personal and dynastic power. This was why Napoleon at times appealed to republican revolutionaries and at other times to those who yearned for the return of a monarchy. The former believed that thanks to him the darkest hours of the French Revolution were behind them, whereas the latter saw in him the person responsible for establishing in France a new civil and economic order. It was Napoleon who for the first time had drafted a strict and precise civil code guaranteeing the rights as well as specifying the duties of a French citizen. He also laid the foundations of a new society based not on an individual's ancestry but on his or her merit. Waterloo marked the end of his reign and the return to power of Louis XVI's brother who tried to restore the monarchy just as it existed before the French Revolution. But the memory and legacy of Napoleon, as Stendhal shows us in his novels, never disappeared and Napoleon has remained to this day a very strong symbol of the French nation. The best example of this is probably the Arc de Triomphe, constructed at one end of the avenue des Champs-Élysées in Paris. It commemorates all Napoleonic victories, just as the arches of triumph in Antiquity commemorated the military victories of Ancient Rome. Each victory of Napoleon's army is engraved in the walls of what is today one of Paris's most famous monuments.
