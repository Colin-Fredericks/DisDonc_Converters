LA STRUCTURE DE LA LANGUE 1: L’EMPLOI DES SUBJONCTIF APRÈS L’EXPRESSION DES SENTIMENTS

Use of the subjunctive is required in subordinate clauses following expressions of emotion. In these cases the subjunctive indicates the speaker’s subjective reaction rather than the objective fact of the occurrence of the action.

Je suis content que tu puisses venir.

I’m glad that you can come.

Il est furieux que vous ne soyez pas prêts.

He’s furious that you’re not ready.

The expression of emotion can be a verb:

Je crains que

I’m afraid that

J’aime que

I like the fact that

or an adjective:

Je suis content que

I’m happy that

or an impersonal expression beginning with il est que or c’est que:

Il est bon que

It’s good that

Il est mauvais que

It’s bad that

Il est triste que

It’s sad that

Il est merveilleux que

It’s wonderful that

Il vaut mieux que

It’s better that

Expressions of emotion are closely related to main clause phrases that express a subjective judgment in that they both employ the subjunctive in the subordinate clause.

Il est important que

It’s important that

Il est essentiel que

It’s essential that

Il est nécessaire que

It’s necessary that

Il est indispensable que

It’s indispensable that

Il est préférable que

It’s preferable that

Il est utile que

It’s useful that

The opposites of these expressions are also followed by the subjunctive:

Il n’est pas nécessaire que

It’s not necessary that

Il est inutile que

It’s useless that

LA STRUCTURE DE LA LANGUE 2: LE FUTUR

The future tense is formed in French by adding a special set of endings to the infinitive of the verb. The r sound appears in all forms of the future tense. Study the future tense of arriver. Listen and repeat the following sentences. Pay special attention to the r sound.

Écoutez.

J’arriverai le mois prochain.

Nous arriverons le mois prochain

I’ll arrive next month.

We’ll arrive next month.

Tu arriveras le mois prochain.

Vous arriverez le mois prochain.

You’ll arrive next month.

You’ll arrive next month.

Il/Elle/On arrivera le mois prochain.

Ils/Elles arriveront le mois prochain.

He/She/One will arrive next month.

They’ll arrive next month.

Now examine the forms of finir, an -ir verb in the future. Listen and repeat.

Ils/Elles finiront leur stage dans un an.

They’ll finish their internship in a year.

Several verbs have irregular stems in the future. In these verbs, the future stem is not derived directly from the infinitive. However, even these irregular stems end in r. The endings are the same for all verbs in the future.

Avoir and Savoir have future stems in aur-:

Avoir

j’aurai, tu auras, etc.

Savoir

je saurai, tu sauras, etc.

Être and Faire have future stems in -er:

Être

je serai, tu seras, etc.

Faire

je ferai, tu feras, etc.

Voir and envoyer have future stems in -err-:

Voir

je verrai, tu verras, etc.

Envoyer

j’enverrai, tu enverras, etc.

Verbs with infinitives in -evoir drop the oi in the future:

Devoir

je devrai, tu devras, etc.

Recevoir

je recevrai, tu recevras, etc.

Courir and mourir drop the i of the infinitive, leaving a double r, which is pronounced in speech.

Courir

je courrai, tu courras, etc.

Other common irregular future stems:

Aller

j’irai, tu iras, etc.

Falloir

il faudra, etc.

Pouvoir

je pourrai, tu pourras, etc.

Soutenir

je soutiendrai, tu soutiendras, etc.

Valoir

il vaudra mieux

Venir

je viendrai, tu viendras, etc.

Vouloir

je voudrai, tu voudras, etc.

Note that verbs like préférer use the infinitive stem in all forms of the future, in spite of the fact that future tense forms have a mute e in the following syllable.

The future tense is not the only tense that expresses future time in French. The present tense can express future time if there are other elements of the sentence that refer to the future.

Écoutez.

Nous partons demain pour la Suisse.

We’re leaving for Switzerland tomorrow.

Écoutez.

On descend à la prochaine station.

We’re getting off at the next station.

Note that English often uses the present progressive (are leaving, are getting off) in these cases.

Aller + Infinitive (le futur proche)

Je vais vous apporter un café.

I’ll bring you a cup of coffee.

Il va s’asseoir dans le salon.

He’s going to sit down in the living room.

Note that in French the aller + infinitive construction and the future tense are not equivalents. The aller + infinitive construction stresses that the action mentioned will soon be completed.

The future tense is less specific in describing the completion of an action. Thus, if a friend at your house says, "J’ai faim," your response should be "Je vais te donner du pain et du fromage". Using the response, "Je te donnerai du pain et du fromage" might make the friend wonder how long he is going to have to wait for something to eat.

When adverbs like maintenant refer to the immediate future, the futur proche must be used.

Maintenant on va manger.

Now we will eat.

However, when an adverb of time referring to the future is used, the futur proche and the future tense are virtually indistinguishable.

On va partir demain

We’ll leave tomorrow.

On partira demain.

We’ll leave tomorrow.

LA STRUCTURE DE LA LANGUE 3: NE . . . QUE

You have already learned the French word seulement, only. Another very common way to say only is to place ne before the verb and que before the word that only qualifies.

Écoutez.

Après, il n’y a que le taxi, et c’est cher.

Afterwards, there’s only the taxi, and it’s expensive.

Baudoin n’a trouvé que deux petits bouts de papier.

Baudoin found only two little pieces of paper.

Je n’ai que ma carte de crédit.

All I have is my credit card.

The structure ne . . . que is not treated as a negative construction, so the partitive article is reduced to de following que.

Écoutez.

Tu as des magazines?

Do you have any magazines?

Non, je n’ai que des journaux.

No, I have only newspapers.

In the absence of a verb, the word que before a noun often means only.

Écoutez.

Il y a du fromage?

Is there any cheese?

Que du pain.

Just bread.

Écoutez.

Tu as des francs?

Do you have any francs?

Que des euros.

Only euros.

Écoutez.

Qu’est-ce que tu as vu dans sa chambre?

What did you see in his room?

Que des livres.

Just books.

Écoutez.

Ils ont des preuves?

Do they have proof?

Que des soupçons.

Just suspicions.

The expression n’avoir qu’à faire qqch means all one has to do is.

Écoutez.

Qu’est-ce qu’il faut faire pour connaître les horaires du TGV?

What do you have to do to find out the schedules of the TGV?

Tu n’as qu’à taper sncf.com.

All you have to do is type sncf.com.

Écoutez.

Le chef n’est pas satisfait du travail de cet employé.

The boss is not satisfied with this employee’s work.

Il n’a qu’à le mettre à la porte.

He should just fire him.

LA STRUCTURE DE LA LANGUE 4: L’HEURE OFFICIELLE

In formal circumstances, (train, bus and air schedules, and event times) French uses a twenty-four hour clock. This system removes the need for using AM and PM. In the twenty-four hour clock, 12:01 o'clock noon is 12h and midnight is 24h. Here are some examples of the use of the 24-hour clock for transportation and movie schedules. Listen and repeat.

Écoutez.

Est-ce qu’il y a un TGV pour Marseille à 14h15?

Is there a TGV to Marseilles at 2:15 PM?

Non, mais il y en a un à 15h30 et un autre à 16h56.

No, but there’s one at 3:30 PM and another at 4:56 PM.

Écoutez.

Est-ce qu’il y a une séance du film à 17h, s'il vous plaît?

Excuse me. Is there a showing of the film at 5:00 PM.

Non, nos séances sont à 19h et à 21h20.

No, we’re showing the film at 7 PM and at 9:20 PM.

Est-ce que la séance de 21h20 est la dernière?

Is the 9:20 PM showing the last one?

Non, aujourd’hui il y a une séance à 23h45.

No, today there is a showing at 11:45 PM.

Does the twenty-four hour clock express minutes from thirty-one after the hour using moins?

Does the twenty-four hour clock designate parts of the hour with et quart, et demie, moins le quart?

If you call an airport in France and you are told, "Le vol (flight) 476 arrive à 9h38," do you ask if the plane is arriving in the morning or evening?

LA STRUCTURE DE LA LANGUE 5: "OÙ" EMPLOYÉ COMME PRONOM RELATIF

The adverb où, where, can also be used as a relative pronoun. Examine the following sentences. Listen and repeat.

Écoutez.

Voilà le guichet où nous avons retiré nos billets.

There is the ticket window where we picked up our tickets.

Voilà l’université où j’ai fait mes études.

There is the university where I studied.

Voilà la corbeille où Baudoin a trouvé les restes d’une enveloppe.

There’s the basket where Baudoin found the remains of an envelope.

Il s’agit de l’année où j’ai commencé à travailler à CyberAvenir.

It’s the year when I began to work at CyberAvenir.

C’est le jour où M. Toësca était absent.

It’s the day when M. Toësca was not at work.

C’était l’après-midi où on a agressé Émile.

It was the afternoon that Émile was attacked.

What two meanings can où have as a relative pronoun?
