Pronounce the following English words aloud. Each contains a vowel followed by a nasal consonant.

If you listen to your pronunciation or to the pronunciation of a friend, you may hear that the nasal consonant is anticipated by making the vowel that precedes it at least partially nasal. A nasal sound is made by lowering the velum, or soft palate, at the back of the mouth to allow air to flow up through the nose. Thus, for most speakers of American English, the velum is lowered during the pronunciation of the vowel that precedes a nasal consonant.

In contrast to English, French vowels before pronounced nasal consonants are completely oral: they do not anticipate the nasal quality of the consonant. Nasal vowels, on the other hand, cannot be followed by a nasal consonant in French.

Structure De La Langue 2: Les Verbes En “-Re”

Verbs ending in re form a separate conjugation in French. In the singular form, their endings are different than verbs whose infinitives end in er. Study the conjugation of descendre, to go down (stairs); to get off, get out (of a vehicle).

je descends
nous descendons
tu descends
vous descendez
il/elle/on descend
ils/elles descendent

The infinitive without the infinitive ending is called the “verb stem.” The stem of descendre is descend.

Note that while the -s ending is added to the first-person and second-person singular (je and tu forms), we add nothing to the third-person singular (il/elle/on).

Here are some other important -re verbs:

attendre
to wait for
entendre
to hear
perdre
to lose
rendre
to give back
répondre
to answer
vendre
to sell

The pronunciation of the forms of descendre reveals an important pattern in spoken French that is not evident in the written language. Although the descend- stem appears in all of the forms of the verb, the stem is pronounced differently in the singular than in the plural.

Listen to the infinitive and to the plural forms of descendre. Note that in each case, the final d of the descend- stem is clearly audible. You will hear each form twice. Repeat each form after the speaker.

descendre
nous descendons
vous descendez
ils descendent

Listen to and repeat the three singular forms of descendre. You will hear each form twice.

je descends
tu descends
il descend

Questions:

  1. How has the stem changed?
  2. Is there any difference in pronunciation among the forms of the singular?
Answers
  1. The final consonant of the stem (in this case, d) is pronounced in the plural verb forms, but dropped in the pronunciation of the singular verb forms.
  2. No, all three forms of the singular are pronounced alike.

The loss of the final consonant of the stem when the singular forms are pronounced is characteristic of all verbs that do not have an infinitive with an -er ending in French. This presence or absence of the final consonant is especially important in the third person (il/elle/on or ils/elles).

What is the difference between the third-person singular and the third-person plural forms in speech?

Answer

The liaison of the final s of ils and elles indicates the plural form.

Answer

The presence of the final d sound of the verb stem indicates a third person plural form. The absence of the final d sound of the stem indicates a third-person singular form.

La Structure de la Langue 4: LA COMPOSITION DE LA PHRASE

Sentences are built around verbs accompanied by other elements. In French, most verbs have a subject that determines the ending of the verb.

Sujet Verbe Subject Verb
Nous marchons We walk
Pierre marche Peter walks

The exact meaning or intent of the verb can be clarified by the addition of elements called “complements,” or completion forms. Complements (not compliments!) complete the idea of the verb. Look at the following sentences.

Peter walks the dog.
Peter walks in the park.
Peter walks away.
Peter walks quickly.
Peter walks to school.

What are the complements in these sentences?

Answer

Complements are boldfaced in the sentences below:

  • Pierre walks the dog.
  • Pierre walks in the park.
  • Pierre walks away.
  • Pierre walks quickly.
  • Pierre walks to school.

Now look at the following sentence: Pierre walks his dog home quickly through the park.

How many complements are there?

What are they?

Answer

There are three complements:

  • There are four complements in the sentence.
  • The complements are: his dog, home, quickly, and through the park.

Simple sentences have one subject and one verb. They may have any number of complements.

Sujet Verbe Complément
Je cherche une chambre meublée.
I am looking for a furnished room.
La chambre donne sur la cour.
The room looks out on the courtyard.

La Structure de la Langue 5: La Phrase Complexe (1): La Subordonnée

Subordinate Clauses

Both French and English have ways of incorporating one sentence into another to make a complex sentence. When a simple sentence is incorporated into a larger sentence, it functions grammatically just as single words function in the simple, original sentence.

After some verbs, for example, a sentence can play the role of the direct object complement. Compare the following two sentences:

Je vois la cour.
I see the courtyard.
Je vois que c’est un vieil immeuble.
I see that it is an old building.

Questions:

  1. What is the direct object in the first sentence?
  2. What is the sentence that functions as the direct object complement of the verb vois (from voir, to see) in the second sentence?
  3. What word links the sentence that functions as the direct object complement to the verb vois (from voir, to see)?
Answers
  1. la cour
  2. C’est un vieil immeuble.
  3. que

The word que is called a “subordinating conjunction.” It joins one sentence to another, and the sentence it joins is subordinate to the main sentence. Thus, the sentence Je vois que c’est un vieil immeuble can be said to have two clauses. The main clause is Je vois and the subordinate clause is introduced by the subordinate conjunction, que. Thus, the subordinate clause is que c’est un vieil immeuble. Sentences that have two or more clauses are called “complex sentences.”

La Structure de la Langue 6: La Phrase Complexe (2): Les Pronoms Relatifs Qui Et Que

Les pronoms relatifs “qui” et “que”

Sentences can modify nouns in the same way adjectives do. Examine the following pairs of sentences:

J’ai une petite chambre trop chère.
J’ai une petite chambre qui coûte les yeux de la tête.
Voici la chambre libre.
Voici la chambre que je vais louer.

Note that in the second sentence of the first pair of sentences, the clause qui coûte les yeux de la tête modifies the noun chambre the same way that chère modifies chambre in the first sentence of the pair. Similarly, in the second sentence of the second pair of sentences, the clause que je vais louer modifies the noun chambre the same way that libre does. Note that the English equivalents work in the same way:

I have an expensive little room.
I have a little room that costs an arm and a leg.
Here’s the empty (= free) room.
Here’s the room that I am going to rent.

A sentence that modifies a noun is called a “relative clause” or an “adjective clause.” The noun that is modified by the relative clause is called the “antecedent.”

Look again at the French sentences:

J’ai une petite chambre qui coûte les yeux de la tête.
Voici la chambre que je vais louer.

What words introduce the relative clauses?

In the relative clause qui coûte les yeux de la tête, is qui the subject or object of the verb coûte?

In the relative clause que je vais louer, is que the subject or object of the verb louer?

Questions:

  1. What words introduce the relative clauses?
  2. In the relative clause qui coûte les yeux de la tête, is qui the subject or object of the verb coûte?
  3. In the relative clause que je vais louer, is que the subject or object of the verb louer?
Answers
  1. The words qui and que introduce the relative clauses. These words are called “relative pronouns.”
  2. The relative pronoun qui is the subject of the verb coûte.
  3. The relative pronoun que is the object of the verb louer.

Now look at the following two sentences:

J’ai un colocataire qui est français.
I have an apartment-mate who is French.
J’ai un colocataire que j’aime bien.
I have an apartment-mate who(m) I like a lot.

Questions:

  1. What is the relative clause in the first sentence?
  2. What noun does the relative clause modify?
  3. What is the relative clause in the second sentence?
  4. What noun does the relative clause modify?
Answers
  1. The relative clause is qui est français.
  2. The relative clause modifies the noun colocataire. Thus, the noun colocataire is the antecedent of the relative clause.
  3. The relative clause is que j’aime bien.
  4. The relative clause modifies the noun colocataire. Thus, the noun colocataire is the antecedent of the relative clause.

  2. In the second set, is the antecedent colocataire an animate or an inanimate noun?
  3. Does the choice of relative pronoun in French (qui vs. que) depend on whether the antecedent is animate or inanimate?
  4. What does the choice of relative pronoun in French depend on?
Answers
  1. The antecedent chambre is an inanimate noun. It refers to a thing.
  2. The antecedent colocataire is an animate noun. It refers to a person.

La Structure de la Langue 8: LES NUMÉROS 60-100

The French system of counting from 60 to 100 is different from the English system. You have seen that both English and French count by tens from 1 to 59. But, from 60 to 99 the French count by twenties. This is a relic of the old Celtic system that survived into French.

More About the Celtic System of Numbers

In the 6th and 7th centuries B.C., the Celts conquered parts of Asia and most of Europe, including what is now France. France was then known as “Gallia,” and although the region was eventually latinized, Celtic words remained part of the French language and are still used today. For example, le berceau, cradle, and brasser, to brew beer, are Celtic words, and several names of French cities, such as Paris, Lyon, Poitiers, and Amiens, are derived from Celtic tribal names or the names of fortified Celtic towns.

Before the Germanic invasion, Britain was also a Celtic-speaking country and, as a result, there are remnants of the Celtic language in English. The English word score reflects the Celtic system of counting by twenties as in four score and seven, which is equivalent to the French quatre-vingt-sept.
