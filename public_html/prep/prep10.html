If you listen to your pronunciation or to the pronunciation of a friend, you may hear that the nasal consonant is anticipated by making the vowel that precedes it at least partially nasal. A nasal sound is made by lowering the velum, or soft palate, at the back of the mouth to allow air to flow up through the nose. Thus, for most speakers of American English, the velum is lowered during the pronunciation of the vowel that precedes a nasal consonant.

In contrast to English, French vowels before pronounced nasal consonants are completely oral: they do not anticipate the nasal quality of the consonant. Nasal vowels, on the other hand, cannot be followed by a nasal consonant in French.

Structure De La Langue 2: Les Verbes En “-Re”

Verbs ending in re form a separate conjugation in French. In the singular form, their endings are different than verbs whose infinitives end in er. Study the conjugation of descendre, to go down (stairs); to get off, get out (of a vehicle).

je descends

nous descendons

tu descends

vous descendez

il/elle/on descend

ils/elles descendent

The infinitive without the infinitive ending is called the “verb stem.” The stem of descendre is descend.

Note that while the -s ending is added to the first-person and second-person singular (je and tu forms), we add nothing to the third-person singular (il/elle/on).

Here are some other important -re verbs:

attendre

to wait for

entendre

to hear

perdre

to lose

rendre

to give back

répondre

to answer

vendre

to sell

The pronunciation of the forms of descendre reveals an important pattern in spoken French that is not evident in the written language. Although the descend- stem appears in all of the forms of the verb, the stem is pronounced differently in the singular than in the plural.

Listen to the infinitive and to the plural forms of descendre. Note that in each case, the final d of the descend- stem is clearly audible. You will hear each form twice. Repeat each form after the speaker.

La Structure de la Langue 5: La Phrase Complexe (1): La Subordonnée

Subordinate Clauses

Both French and English have ways of incorporating one sentence into another to make a complex sentence. When a simple sentence is incorporated into a larger sentence, it functions grammatically just as single words function in the simple, original sentence.

After some verbs, for example, a sentence can play the role of the direct object complement. Compare the following two sentences:

Je vois la cour.

I see the courtyard.

Je vois que c’est un vieil immeuble.

I see that it is an old building.

What is the direct object in the first sentence?

What is the sentence that functions as the direct object complement of the verb vois (from voir, to see) in the second sentence?

What word links the sentence that functions as the direct object complement to the verb vois (from voir, to see)?

Answers

The word que is called a “subordinating conjunction.” It joins one sentence to another, and the sentence it joins is subordinate to the main sentence. Thus, the sentence Je vois que c’est un vieil immeuble can be said to have two clauses. The main clause is Je vois and the subordinate clause is introduced by the subordinate conjunction, que. Thus, the subordinate clause is que c’est un vieil immeuble. Sentences that have two or more clauses are called “complex sentences.”

La Structure de la Langue 6: La Phrase Complexe (2): Les Pronoms Relatifs Qui Et Que

Les pronoms relatifs “qui” et “que”

Sentences can modify nouns in the same way adjectives do. Examine the following pairs of sentences:

J’ai une petite chambre trop chère.

J’ai une petite chambre qui coûte les yeux de la tête.

Voici la chambre libre.

Voici la chambre que je vais louer.

Note that in the second sentence of the first pair of sentences, the clause qui coûte les yeux de la tête modifies the noun chambre the same way that chère modifies chambre in the first sentence of the pair. Similarly, in the second sentence of the second pair of sentences, the clause que je vais louer modifies the noun chambre the same way that libre does. Note that the English equivalents work in the same way:

I have an expensive little room.

I have a little room that costs an arm and a leg.

Here’s the empty (= free) room.

Here’s the room that I am going to rent.

A sentence that modifies a noun is called a “relative clause” or an “adjective clause.” The noun that is modified by the relative clause is called the “antecedent.”

Look again at the French sentences:

J’ai une petite chambre qui coûte les yeux de la tête.

Note the following:

The connecting word et is used in soixante-et-un and soixante-et-onze but not in quatre-vingt-un and quatre-vingt-onze.

Note on Numerals Over 100:

The connecting word et is not used after forms of cent, the way and may be used in English: cent un, one hundred and one; cent soixante-dix, one hundred and seventy.

The s of deux cents, trois cents, etc., undergoes liaison, becoming a z sound before a noun beginning with a vowel: trois cents écoles, six cents hommes.

The s of deux cents, trois cents, etc., drops if the word appears before another number: deux cent quatre-vingt quatre, cinq cent soixante-seize.

The number un does not appear before cent and mille the way one does in English: one hundred = cent, one thousand = mille
