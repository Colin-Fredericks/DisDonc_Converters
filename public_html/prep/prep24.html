3. Prononciation

Question Intonation: Questions beginning with an Interrogative

In English and French, questions that begin with an interrogative have falling, not rising intonation. However, the pattern is somewhat different in the two languages. In English, these questions begin at a middle-level pitch on the question word, rise to high pitch on the stressed syllable and then drop. Listen to the speaker read the following English questions.

  • What are you doing?
  • Where are you going?
  • Where is he from?
  • Why is he crying?
  • When does the movie begin?
  • At what time did the plane arrive?
  • How do you pronounce this?
  • Who’s knocking at the door?

In French, however, these questions begin at a high pitch on the interrogative and then there is a syllable-by-syllable drop in the pitch to the end of the sentence. Repeat after the speaker.

  • Qu’est-ce que vous faites?
  • Où est-ce que vous allez?
  • D’où est-il?
  • Pourquoi il pleure?
  • Quand commence le film?
  • À quelle heure est-ce que l’avion est arrivé?
  • Comment est-ce que tu prononces ça?
  • Qui frappe à la porte?

La Structure De La Langue 5: Les Substantifs Qui Remplacent Une Proposition

French often uses a noun where English uses a subordinate clause. When a subordinate clause in English expresses motion, French stylistic usage often prefers a noun phrase.

Je l’ai vu à son retour.
I saw him when he got back.

Nous attendons votre arrivée.
We’re waiting for you to get here.

Questions:

  1. In the above sentences, which nouns express motion?
  2. How do the English sentences express the same ideas?
  3. What is the French equivalent of the subjects of the verbs of motion found in the English sentences?
  4. What takes the place of the conjunction "when" in the French sentence?
Show Answers
  1. French sentences use the nouns retour and arrivée.
  2. English uses the expressions when he got back and you to get here.
  3. The possessive adjective son is the equivalent of the subject he in the first English sentence. The possessive adjective votre is the equivalent of the subject you in the second English sentence.
  4. The equivalent of the English conjunction "when" is the preposition à. This is logical because a conjunction is followed by the clause when he got back, but a noun is introduced by the preposition: à son retour.
