Vocabulary Note

The official English translation for la charcuterie is the pork butcher's. In the past, le charcutier specialized in sausages while le boucher sold fresh meat. Today's charcuterie specializes not only in sausages but in ready-made foods such as salads, quiches, and soufflés as well as luncheon meats. The charcuterie is sometimes combines with the traiteur, caterer, who specializes in elaborate prepared dishes for business lunches, receptions, and dinners at home.

La Structure De La Langue 1: Quantité

Many words indicating quantity in French are joined to the nouns they modify by the preposition de.

Containers and weights often serve as quantity words.

Écoutez.

un kilo de pommes de terre

a kilogram of potatoes

une bouteille de limonade

a bottle of soda

une boîte de bonbons

a box of candy

The placement of de after quantity words is invariable: de does not combine with the definite article and is therefore used before singular and plural nouns of both genders. It does, however, change to d’ before a vowel.

Here are some other, commonly used quantity words followed by de.

Écoutez.

beaucoup de viande

a lot of meat

beaucoup de pommes de terre

a lot of potatoes

assez de chocolat

enough chocolate

assez d’imprimantes

enough printers

trop d’effort

too much effort

trop de messages

too many messages

un peu de vin

a little wine

peu de vin

little (= not much) wine

peu d’amis

few friends

The phrase pas mal de is used in everyday French as a synonym for beaucoup de. Both phrases mean a lot of.

Écoutez.

pas mal d’argent

a lot of money

pas mal d’affiches

a lot of posters

Note also:

une douzaine d’œufs

a dozen eggs

La Structure De La Langue 2: Le Présent De “Connaître”

The verb connaître means to know a person or to be familiar with a place or subject. In written French, the singular has the expected endings s, s, t, while the plural forms are all written with ss before the endings. In spoken French, the final consonant sound of the stem is s (represented by ss in the written forms). This s sound is pronounced in the plural forms, but drops in the singular. Concentrate on the pronunciation of the forms of the present tense of connaître. Listen and repeat.

Écoutez.

Je connais le charcutier.

Nous connaissons le charcutier.

Tu connais le charcutier.

Vous connaissez le charcutier.

Il/Elle/On connaît le charcutier.

Ils/Elles connaissent le charcutier.

Note that with place names, connaître can often be translated as have been to.

Écoutez.

Tu connais le Quartier Latin?

Do you know the Latin Quarter?

Non, je ne connais pas Paris.

No, I’ve never been to Paris.

La Structure De La Langue 3: Le Présent De “Savoir”

The verb savoir, to know, shows a pattern in its conjugation that is typical of verbs whose infinitives do not end in er. Study the forms of the present tense of savoir. Listen and repeat.

Écoutez.

Je sais son nom.

Nous savons son nom.

Tu sais son nom.

Vous savez son nom.

Il/Elle/On sait son nom.

Ils/Elles savent son nom.

What are the written endings of the singular? How do they compare with the endings of connaître?

Pronounce the forms of the plural after the speaker. What is the final consonant sound of the stem of the verb savoir?

What happens to that final consonant sound in the spoken forms of the singular? Is there any other change in the singular forms?

Answers

Note that in the negative of savoir, the mute e of ne drops after the pronouns je, tu, on, nous, and vous. The same is true in the negative of connaître. Listen and repeat, with special attention to how the speaker pronounces ne.

Écoutez.

Je ne sais pas.

Tu ne sais pas.

On ne sait pas.

Nous ne savons pas.

Vous ne savez pas.

Écoutez.

Je ne connais pas.

Tu ne connais pas.

On ne connaît pas.

Nous ne connaissons pas.

Vous ne connaissez pas.

Écoutez.

Il ne sait pas.

Elles ne connaissent pas.

L'emploi de “savoir” et “connaître”

Savoir means to know in the sense of to know facts or information, to possess knowledge.

Écoutez.

Tu sais son adresse?

Do you know her address?

Vous savez l’heure?

Do you know the time?

Ils ne savent pas le prix du repas.

They don’t know the price of the meal.

Savoir is usually used before a clause. The clause may begin with que or with a question word.

Écoutez.

Je ne sais pas où elle habite.

I don’t know where she lives.

Nous savons qu’elle habite à Paris.

We know she lives in Paris.

Tu sais quand elle revient?

Do you know when she’s coming back?

Yes/no questions become clauses beginning with si, whether, if, after savoir.

Écoutez.

Question:

Est-ce qu’elle travaille ici?

Does she work here?

Clause:

Je ne sais pas si elle travaille ici.

I don’t know whether she works here or not.

The verb connaître expresses familiarity. It is usually followed by a noun referring to a person or place and rarely followed by a clause.

Écoutez.

Je ne connais pas tes cousines.

I don’t know your cousins.

On connaît bien Paris.

We know Paris well.

La Structure De La Langue 4: Il Faut + L'Infinitif

Il faut is known as the expression of obligation. Look at the following sentence from the Conversation: “C’est comme ça qu’il faut finir un repas!”

Answers

La Structure De La Langue 5: Le Présent De “Devoir”

The verb devoir, should, ought, have to, is irregular in the present tense. Listen and repeat.

Écoutez.

Je dois faire un effort.

Nous devons faire un effort.

Tu dois faire un effort.

Vous devez faire un effort.

Il/Elle/On doit faire un effort.

Ils/Elles doivent faire un effort.

Is devoir a verb with a variable stem? If so, what is the variation in the stem?

What is the last consonant of the stem?

What are the endings of the singular?

Show Answers

Answers

Devoir is a verb with a variable stem. The infinitive and nous and vous forms have a mute e: devoir, nous devons, vous devez. The singular forms and the third-person plural forms have the vowel oi: je dois, tu dois, il doit, ils doivent. Compare the variations in the verbs acheter and vouloir:

Acheter

nous achetons, vous achetez

vs.

j’achète tu achètes, il achète, ils achètent

Vouloir

nous voulons, vous voulez

vs.

je veux, tu veux, il veut, ils veulent

The last consonant of the stem is v. The v is heard in the plural forms but drops in the singular.

The endings of the singular are the expected endings for verbs whose infinitive does not end in er: s, s, t.

Devoir expresses obligation and is the equivalent of English should, ought to, must. In these meanings it is followed by an infinitive.

Qu’est-ce que je dois faire?

What should I do?

Tu dois parler avec le professeur.

You ought to speak with the teacher.

In the negative, devoir means mustn’t, shouldn’t.

Tu ne dois pas faire ça.

You musn’t/shouldn’t do that.

Note that with devoir, the person who has the obligation must be expressed, while il faut + infinitive is used to state a general obligation.

Vous devez répondre en français.

You must answer in French.

Il faut répondre en français.

It is necessary to answer in French.

La Structure De La Langue 6: Le Présent De “Mettre”

Study the forms of mettre, to put. Listen and repeat.

Écoutez.

Je mets le pain sur la table.

Nous mettons le pain sur la table.

Tu mets le pain sur la table.

Vous mettez le pain sur la table.

Il/Elle/On met le pain sur la table.

Ils/Elles mettent le pain sur la table.

How does the stem of the verb end in the infinitive and the plural forms?

There is a slight difference between the usual written endings of the singular of verbs whose infinitive does not end in re and those of mettre. What is that difference and why does it occur?

Show Answers

Answers

The stem of mettre ends in t. This t is not pronounced in the singular forms.

There is no t added in the third-person singular because the written stem already ends in t: met.

The vowel pronounced in the stem of mettre varies depending on the region of France and on the speaker. Some people use open è in all forms. (All speakers say è in ils mettent.) Some people use an open è in the singular and third person plural, but a closed é in nous mettons and vous mettez. Others use a closed é in all forms except ils mettent.

La Structure De La Langue 8: L'Heure

To tell time in French, use il est + the number of the hour + the word heure/heures.

Écoutez.

Il est une heure.

It’s one o’clock.

Il est deux heures.

It’s two o’clock.

Il est onze heures.

It’s eleven o’clock.

Il est midi.

It’s twelve o’clock (noon).

Il est minuit.

It’s twelve o’clock (midnight).

Note the following:

The singular, heure, is used for une heure, one o’clock. The plural, heures, is used for the other hours.

The sentence Il est douze heures. for It’s twelve o’clock. is not used in this style of telling time. It is replaced by Il est midi/minuit.

Minutes after the hour are added directly to the base sentence.

Écoutez.

Il est une heure cinq.

It’s five after one.

Il est trois heures dix.

It’s ten after three.

Il est sept heures vingt.

It’s twenty after seven.

Remember that midi and minuit are used for twelve o’clock.

Écoutez.

Il est midi et quart.

It’s twelve fifteen (p.m.).

Il est minuit et demie.

It’s twelve thirty (a.m.).

The number of minutes from 29 to the following hour are expressed with the word moins, minus:

Écoutez.

Il est huit heures moins vingt-cinq.

It’s twenty-five to eight.

Il est midi moins dix.

It’s ten to twelve (p.m.).

Il est une heure moins cinq.

It’s five to one.

For a quarter after the hour, the phrase et quart, and a quarter, is used.

Écoutez.

Il est deux heures et quart.

It’s a quarter after two.

Il est minuit et quart.

It’s a quarter after twelve (a.m.).

For half past the hour, the phrase et demie, and a half, is used.

Écoutez.

Il est cinq heures et demie.

It’s half-past five.

Il est neuf heures et demie.

It’s half-past nine.

For a quarter to the hour, the phrase moins le quart,minus a quarter, is used:

Écoutez.

Il est minuit moins le quart.

It’s a quarter to twelve (midnight).

Il est dix heures moins le quart.

It’s a quarter to ten.

The use of digital clocks has made counting the minutes from 1 to 59 more common, as in current English.

Écoutez.

Il est quatre heures quinze.

It’s four fifteen.

Il est quatre heures trente.

It’s four thirty.

Il est quatre heures quarante-cinq.

It’s four forty-five.

Il est quatre heures cinquante.

It’s four fifty.

To ask what time it is, the formal question is as follows:

Écoutez.

Quelle heure est-il?

In everyday speech you will often hear a different question:

Écoutez.

Il est quelle heure?

To ask or tell at what time something takes place, the preposition à is used.

Écoutez.

À quelle heure est-ce qu’il arrive?

What time is he arriving?

Il arrive à neuf heures.

He’s arriving at nine thirty.

Note that the French equivalent of a.m. and p.m. are the phrases du matin, in the morning, de l’après-midi, in the afternoon, and du soir,in the evening.

Écoutez.

Il est dix heures du matin.

It’s ten a.m.

Il est dix heures du soir.

It’s ten p.m.

Il est une heure de l’après-midi.

It’s one p.m.

Il est une heure du matin.

It’s one a.m.

These phrases are of course not used after midi,noon, or minuit, midnight.

The following phrases are used to describe the accuracy of a timepiece.

Écoutez.

Ma montre avance.

My watch is fast.

Ma montre avance de cinq minutes.

My watch is five minutes fast.

Ma montre retarde.

My watch is slow.

Ma montre retarde de cinq minutes.

My watch is five minutes slow.

Ma montre est exacte.

My watch is correct.
