Langue Et Culture

À gauche

Traditional French apartment houses have several staircases, and each landing has two apartments, one on the left and one on the right. The location of an apartment is described by its floor and whether it is to the left or the right of the staircase: Elle habite au quatrième étage, à gauche, means She lives in the apartment to the left on the fourth floor.

Au premier étage

The first floor of a French building is called le premier étage and is usually one flight up. The street-level floor is called le rez-de-chaussée. Thus, Élaine’s apartment au quatrième étage would be on the fifth floor in the United States.

Le week-end

When a word or phrase is borrowed from another language it can be changed in form without reference to its language of origin. For example, weekend is spelled with a hyphen in French, le week-end. Similary, the English football becomes le football in French and has developed a slang form, le foot. Thus, a French sentence such as “J’aime le foot.” borrows from an English word but in a way that most speakers of English would not understand.

The Days of the Week

Note that the days of the week are not written with capital letters in French. On French calenders, Monday is the first day of the week, Sunday the last. The first letters of the French days of the week are used as abbreviations for lundi, mardi, mercredi, jeudi, vendredi, samedi, dimanche: L, M, M, J, V, S, D.

The French sound represented by ou is produced with the tongue arched high in the mouth, with the top of the arch at the back of the mouth. The lips are rounded and protruding. La Structure de la Langue 1: LES VERBES EN CER

You have seen that verbs ending in ger, like manger to eat have a spelling irregularity in the nous form: nous mangeons. The e is added because of the rules of French spelling, which use g before a, o, or u to represent the hard sound of g (the g of English go), and ge or gi to represent the soft sound of g, a sound similar to the s of English pleasure.

Similarly, verbs ending in cer like commencer to begin change c to ç (c with a cédille) to represent the soft sound of the letter c before the letters a and o. Thus, you write nous commençons with ç because the -ons ending begins with the letter o.

La Structure de la Langue 3: ALLER + INFINITIVE

The verb aller may be followed by an infinitive to express the idea of a future action. The English construction going to do something expresses a similar idea.

Je vais frapper. I’m going to knock.
Nous allons monter l’escalier. We are going to go up the stairs.
Tu vas être complètement essoufflé. You’re going to be all out of breath.

In the negative of the aller + infinitive construction, ne precedes the form of the verb aller and pas follows it. The negation of aller comes before the infinitive:

Je ne vais pas entrer. I’m not going to go in.
Il ne va pas être en retard. He’s not going to be late.
Ils ne vont pas regarder la télé. They’re not going to watch TV.

Note that the infinitive in this aller + infinitive construction can also be aller, as in English going to go somewhere.

Tu vas aller au match avec moi? Are you going to go to the game with me?

La Structure de la Langue 4: QUESTION FORMATION WITH EST-CE QUE

Read the following French questions:

Elle habite au troisième étage? Does she live on the third floor?
Tu as un grand appartement? Do you have a big apartment?
What change was made to the statements “Elle habite au troisième étage.” and “Tu as un grand appartement.” to turn them into questions?

Was there any change in word order or were elements added to the statements to turn them into questions?

Answers

Questions formed solely by changing the statement intonation to question intonation are typical in informal French. In more formal speech, the phrase est-ce que precedes the statement in order to turn it into a question:

Est-ce qu’elle habite au troisième étage? Does she live on the third floor?
Est-ce que tu as un grand appartement? Do you have a large apartment?

Note that the final e of que is elided before a vowel and replaced by an apostrophe in spelling. Among the pronouns, this élision occurs before il, elle, ils, and elles.

Remember that statements can also be turned into questions by adding n’est-ce pas? at the end:

Il y a un ascenseur, n’est-ce pas? There’s an elevator, isn’t there?
Nous allons en voiture, n’est-ce pas? We’re going by car, aren’t we?

Note that questions formed with n’est-ce pas? imply that the speaker thinks the information is true and expects a confirmation.

La Structure de la Langue 5: LE PRONOM On

The pronoun on is used when speakers want to stress the action expressed by the verb rather than the person performing the action. The English equivalents of on are one, you, they, people, or the passive voice. The subject pronoun on is followed by a third-person singular verb, the same form used with il and elle.

On mange bien en France. People eat well/You eat well in France.
On entre par cette porte. You enter/One enters through this door.
On ne joue pas au baseball en France. Baseball is not played in France.
Ici on parle français. French is spoken here.

In spoken French the pronoun on replaces nous. The verb with on is still third-person singular even when on means we.

Où est-ce qu’on va ce weekend? Where are we going this weekend?
On passe le weekend à Paris. We’re spending the weekend in Paris.

The stressed pronoun used with on is nous.

Moi, je travaille à la bibliothèque. Et vous? I study at the library. How about you?
Nous, on travaille dans notre chambre. We study in our room.

The use of on instead of nous is characteristic of informal language.

La Structure de la Langue 6: LES MOTS INTERROGATIFS

Question words and phrases such as “where?” or “how much?” are used to ask for information. The most common French question words and phrases are as follows:

Où? Where?
Pourquoi? Why?
Comment? How?
Qui? Who? or Whom?
Qu’est-ce que . . . ? What?
Quand? When?

The question word combien, meaning how many or how much, is followed by de when it appears before a noun.

Combien d’étages? How many floors?

The question word quel always precedes a noun and agrees with it in gender and number. Listen and repeat:

Quel musée? Which museum?
Quelle porte? Which door?
Quels musées? Which museums?
Quelles portes? Which doors?

Is there any difference in pronunciation in the four forms of quel?

Answer

Quelle université? Which university?

La Structure de la Langue 7: LES MOTS INTERROGATIFS

Compare the following French and English sentences:

D’où est-ce que vous arrivez? Where are you arriving from?
Par où est-ce qu’on entre? Where do we go in through?
Avec qui est-ce que tu étudies? Who(m) do you study with?
À quel étage est-ce que tu habites? What floor do you live on?
The first words in the French sentences are prepositions. Where are the prepositions placed in French word order?

Where do the prepositions appear in the English sentences?

Can you formulate a rule to describe the difference in position of prepositions in English and French sentences?

Answers

When there is a preposition, it is common to drop est-ce que and place the preposition and question word at the end of the sentence.

D’où est-ce que vous arrivez? Vous arrivez d’où?
Par où est-ce qu’on entre? On entre par où?
Avec qui est-ce que tu étudies? Tu étudies avec qui?
À quel étage est-ce que tu habites? Tu habites à quel étage?

Note, however, that even in this pattern the preposition still precedes its object in French. It does not come at the end of the sentence as in English.

La Structure de la Langue 8: SI VS. OUI

There are two ways to answer “yes” to a question in French. Oui is used to answer affirmative questions. Si, on the other hand, is used to answer “yes” to a negative question.

Il n’y a pas d’ascenseur?
Si, mais il est en panne.
