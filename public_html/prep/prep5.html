Langue Et Culture 1

Euro

The euro is the new currency of the European Union, which replaced existing currencies for the majority of member nations. The previous French currency was the franc, official since 1796. Though the exchange rate varies, one euro is roughly equivalent to one U.S. dollar.

Amis

French has two words for friend: l’ami(e) and le copain (la copine). There are several differences between the two. The term copain/copine is a more informal word, characteristic of the speech of students and young people. The term un ami/une amie refers to a very close friend. The word copain/copine can refer to more casual friendships, and les copains can refer to a circle of friends. One can have any number of copains, but very few amis.

Le service

Le service is the service charge or tip in a restaurant or café. In France, a service charge is usually added to the bill, but not always, so it is good to ask: Le service est compris? Customers may choose to leave some additional change for the server.

La Structure De La Langue 1

Plurals of Nouns and Articles

To indicate that a noun is plural, s is added to the singular:

Plural

euros

However, in contrast with English, the final s, like most final consonants in French, is not sounded. Thus, you cannot tell whether a noun is singular or plural just by hearing it in isolation.

In spoken French, the determiner indicates whether the noun is singular or plural. Look at the forms of the definite article.

Singular
Plural
le voisin

le billet
les billets
la calculette

The plural of both le and la is les. It is especially important to distinguish among the three definite articles in speech. The distinction between le and la signals the gender of the noun (masculine or feminine). The distinction between le, la, and les signals whether the noun is singular or plural.

5. Pronunciation Review

Definite Articles

The vowel of le is called a “schwa,” and it also occurs in the second syllable of English sofa. When fully pronounced, the “schwa” is similar to the eu of peu, européen, merveilleux, and heureux. However, under certain conditions, the “schwa” in French disappears. This “schwa” is usually called “mute e” because it is silent in certain positions.

La is pronounced with a clear vowel sound — one similar to the vowel in standard American English hat. The a of la should not be pronounced as a schwa.

Before a noun beginning with a consonant, the plural definite article, les is pronounced as if it were written lé. Les should not be pronounced as a schwa.

Nouns Beginning with a Vowel

Remember that before a noun beginning with a vowel, both le and la undergo élision and become l’:

l’euro

l’imprimante

Before a plural noun beginning with a vowel, the final s of les undergoes liaison, and is linked to the following word and pronounced like the z in English zebra.

les euros (s of les pronounced as English z)

les imprimantes (s of les pronounced as English z)

La Structure De La Langue 5

Use of “de” in Compound Nouns

Compare the following phrase in French and English:

une carte de crédit
Credit card

Questions

  1. How does English form a compound consisting of two nouns?
  2. How does French form a compound consisting of two nouns?
  3. How does the word order differ in French?
Show Answers
  1. One of the nouns is placed directly in front of the other. The head noun (the basic part of the phrase) comes second; the modifying noun comes first. Card is basic; credit (the modifying noun) describes the kind of card.
  2. French connects the two nouns by de.
  3. The head noun (carte) comes first. The modifying noun (crédit) comes second, connected by de.

Note that sometimes other prepositions, such as à, are used to form compound nouns that label the main ingredient or characteristic feature:

un sandwich au fromage
a cheese sandwich
une glace à la fraise
strawberry ice cream

To make these compound nouns plural, an s is added only to the first noun.

un carte de crédit
des cartes de crédit

In some compound nouns the second noun is always plural.

une tarte aux pommes
des tartes aux pommes

Pronunciation of Numbers

In French, numbers present several complications in their pronunciation. As you read through this description, click on the audio buttons to hear the pronunciation of the phrases that contain the numbers. Each of the phrases will be read twice with pauses for repetition.

The forms for the number one in French are the same as the indefinite article. Therefore, un andune, one, agree in gender with the following nouns:

J’ai un franc.
I have one franc.
J’ai une copine à Paris.
I have one friend in Paris.

The final x of six or dix is pronounced. However, when these numbers stand before a noun, the x is not pronounced.

six cafés
dix francs

The final t of sept and the final f of neuf are always pronounced:

sept euros
sept restaurants
neuf sandwichs

The final q of cinq is pronounced when counting, but drops before a noun beginning with a consonant:

cinq garçons

Liaison with Numbers

The r of quatre is pronounced very faintly before a noun beginning with a consonant. This is often inaudible in rapid speech.

quatre cafés

The r of quatre is pronounced when the next word begins with a vowel.

quatre express

The final s of trois and the final x of deux, six, and dix are linked before a noun beginning with a vowel.

deux euros
trois euros
six euros
dix euros

The final f of neuf is linked before a noun beginning with a vowel.

neuf euros

28. Read the Note Culturelle (Cultural Note) on Economic Globalization.

Note Culturelle

French Fears of Economic Globalization

For quite a few years now, the French have been expressing distrust and skepticism concerning the theoretical benefits of economic globalization as defined in classic textbooks on international economics. Indeed, in a country very widely affected by unemployment since the end of the 1980s - even at the start of the year 2000, France's unemployment rate hovered around 11% - economic globalization means heightened competition and the shifting of economic activity to countries where lower wages are the norm. Numerous sectors of the French economy, such as the steel industry, have had to undergo painful reforms, and certain others, such as the textile and shipbuilding industries, have practically disappeared. Furthermore, the French fear that the increase in the volume and speed of global exchanges will call into question France's national sovereignty. In a country so proud of its traditions and culture, it is a particular way of life more than anything, that the modern economic structure threatens, and the dispute is becoming more intense all the time.

In the cultural realm, the growing domination of the English language and of American cultural products has repeatedly forced the French government to take draconian measures to encourage the use of the French language and support French creative initiatives. Since the mid-1980s, quotas have been imposed on French television networks and radio stations, and an original system for the funding of French film productions was established in order to prevent the total disappearance of French cinema. The Académie française, a body of French language experts that determines French linguistic standards, regularly suggests alternative terms in French for English words and expressions (such as "start-up" or "Web," for example) and the use of these terms is sometimes subsequently mandated by law.

France recently found an anti-globalization hero in a sheep breeder named José Bové who made himself known when he led an assault in August 1999 to ransack a McDonald'sä restaurant that was being constructed. Indeed, this fast-food chain was targeted as a symbol of the United States and of what Mr. Bové calls "the bad food multinationals." A series of problems associated with food and health (the Mad Cow epidemic in Great Britain, dioxin-contaminated chickens in Belgium, etc.) made consumers very sensitive to this topic and resulted in a generalized anguish over any type of manipulation of nature. It is this mentality that urged Europe to maintain its prohibition of imports of beef treated with growth hormone. This action led to the July 1999 U.S. decision to impose customs duties of 100% on certain European food products, among them Roquefort cheese and foie gras, products symbolic of France and very dear to the French. Mr. Bové's rebellion was primarily a reaction to the loss inflicted on small producers of Roquefort.

Today it is the Internet that best embodies the ideal of a planet without borders, and therefore new technologies, which have the potential to dangerously infringe on certain aspects of private life, have naturally also become an object of debate. The French have enacted several laws aiming to limit if not prohibit the circulation of information about private life with any country deemed incapable of offering sufficient protection from potential infraction.

At the dawn of the 21st century, the French are asking themselves how to best preserve political primacy over the economy, a difficult challenge indeed.
